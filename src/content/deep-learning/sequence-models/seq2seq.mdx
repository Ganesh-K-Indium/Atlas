## Intuition

Translation, summarization, and speech recognition share a structural problem that plain classification doesn't have: the input and output are both sequences, and they can be **different lengths** and have **no fixed alignment** — "I am a student" (4 words) becomes "Je suis étudiant" (3 words) in French, and word order doesn't map one-to-one. A model that must output exactly one label per input token (like a tagger) can't handle this. You need something that reads the whole input first, then generates an output sequence of whatever length is appropriate.

<Callout type="tip" title="The one-sentence idea">
  Seq2Seq splits the job into two networks: an **encoder** that reads the entire input and compresses it into a representation, and a **decoder** that generates the output sequence one token at a time, conditioned on that representation and on its own previous outputs.
</Callout>

## The Original Architecture: One Vector to Rule Them All

The original Seq2Seq formulation (Sutskever, Vinyals & Le, 2014) uses two RNNs (in practice, LSTMs):

1. The **encoder** processes the source sequence $x_1, \ldots, x_n$ step by step, updating its hidden state as usual, and keeps *only the final hidden state* $h_n$ — call it the **context vector** $c$.
2. The **decoder**, another RNN, is initialized with $c$ and generates the target sequence $y_1, \ldots, y_m$ autoregressively: at each step it takes its own previous output $y_{t-1}$ and hidden state, produces a distribution over the vocabulary for $y_t$, samples or greedily picks a token, and feeds it back in as input to the next step.

$$
P(y_1, \ldots, y_m \mid x_1, \ldots, x_n) = \prod_{t=1}^{m} P\big(y_t \mid y_1, \ldots, y_{t-1}, \, c\big)
$$

Training uses **teacher forcing**: during training, the decoder is fed the *true* previous token $y_{t-1}$ from the training data (not its own possibly-wrong prediction), which stabilizes and speeds up learning; at inference time it has no choice but to feed back its own generated tokens.

## The Fixed-Context-Vector Bottleneck

This design has an obvious structural flaw once you look closely: **the entire source sentence must be compressed into a single fixed-size vector $c$**, regardless of whether the sentence has 5 words or 50. The decoder never looks back at the source sequence again after that first step — everything it knows about the input has to survive the compression into $c$ and then get carried, unchanged in content, across every decoding step.

Two consequences follow directly:

- **Long sentences degrade disproportionately.** As $n$ grows, more information has to be squeezed into the same fixed-size $c$, and empirically translation quality drops sharply for longer source sentences — the exact symptom you'd expect from a fixed-capacity bottleneck.
- **The decoder has no way to "look up" which part of the source is relevant to the word it's generating right now.** When generating the French word for "student," ideally the model would re-examine the region of the input near "student" — but $c$ has already discarded that positional distinction; it's one blended summary.

This is structurally the same kind of information bottleneck as the vanishing-gradient problem in plain RNNs (see [RNNs, LSTMs & GRUs](/docs/deep-learning/sequence-models/rnn-lstm-gru)): a single fixed-size intermediate is being asked to carry more information than it can represent well as sequence length grows.

## Fixing It: Attention Over Encoder States

Bahdanau, Cho & Bengio (2014) proposed the fix that turned out to be one of the most consequential ideas in deep learning: **instead of discarding all but the final encoder hidden state, keep every encoder hidden state $h_1, \ldots, h_n$, and let the decoder look back at all of them at every decoding step**, weighting them by relevance to what it's currently generating.

Concretely, at decoder step $t$, with decoder hidden state $s_t$:

1. Compute an **alignment score** between the decoder's current state and every encoder hidden state:
$$
e_{t,i} = \text{score}(s_t, h_i)
$$
   (Bahdanau used a small feedforward network on $[s_t, h_i]$; Luong's later variant used a simpler dot product or bilinear form — the functional role is identical.)
2. Normalize the scores into a probability distribution over source positions with softmax:
$$
\alpha_{t,i} = \frac{\exp(e_{t,i})}{\sum_{j=1}^{n} \exp(e_{t,j})}
$$
3. Form a **context vector specific to this decoding step** as a weighted sum of all encoder states:
$$
c_t = \sum_{i=1}^{n} \alpha_{t,i} \, h_i
$$
4. Feed $c_t$ (concatenated with the decoder's own state) into the output layer to produce $y_t$.

The critical change from the original formulation: **the context vector is no longer fixed — it's recomputed at every decoder step**, and it's a weighted combination over the *entire* source sequence rather than a single compressed summary. The decoder effectively gets to "re-read" the source, focusing on different words as it generates different output words — which is exactly the soft-alignment behavior you'd want for translation, and it's visualizable: plotting $\alpha_{t,i}$ as a heatmap shows the model learning roughly monotonic (and sometimes reordered) word alignments between languages, learned entirely from data with no explicit alignment supervision.

<Callout type="note" title="This is the direct ancestor of self-attention">
  The Bahdanau/Luong attention mechanism above — query = decoder state, keys/values = encoder states, softmax-weighted sum — is *exactly* the Query/Key/Value pattern used in the Transformer's [self-attention](/docs/deep-learning/attention-transformers/self-attention-multi-head), generalized from "decoder attends to a separate encoder" (cross-attention) to "every token attends to every other token in the same sequence" (self-attention). See [The Transformer Architecture](/docs/deep-learning/attention-transformers/transformer-architecture) for how removing the recurrence entirely, while keeping this attention mechanism as the *only* way information moves between positions, led to the modern Transformer.
</Callout>

## Complexity

| Component | Time (attention-augmented Seq2Seq) | Memory | Sequential constraint |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Encoder (RNN) | $O(n \cdot d^2)$ | $O(n \cdot d)$ to keep all hidden states | Sequential over $n$ |
| Attention per decoder step | $O(n \cdot d)$ | $O(n)$ scores | — |
| Full decode | $O(m \cdot n \cdot d)$ total for attention across all steps | $O(n \cdot d)$ encoder cache | Sequential over $m$ (autoregressive) |

Attention adds an $O(n)$ cost per decoding step (attending over all $n$ encoder positions) but removes the information bottleneck entirely — a trade found to be overwhelmingly worth it, and one that generalizes directly to the Transformer's $O(n^2)$ self-attention, discussed in [The Transformer Architecture](/docs/deep-learning/attention-transformers/transformer-architecture).

## Code Sketch

```python
import torch
import torch.nn as nn
import torch.nn.functional as F

class Encoder(nn.Module):
    def __init__(self, vocab_size, emb_dim, hidden_dim):
        super().__init__()
        self.embed = nn.Embedding(vocab_size, emb_dim)
        self.rnn = nn.LSTM(emb_dim, hidden_dim, batch_first=True)

    def forward(self, src):
        embedded = self.embed(src)
        outputs, (h_n, c_n) = self.rnn(embedded)
        return outputs, (h_n, c_n)   # keep ALL outputs, not just h_n


class BahdanauAttention(nn.Module):
    def __init__(self, hidden_dim):
        super().__init__()
        self.attn = nn.Linear(hidden_dim * 2, hidden_dim)
        self.v = nn.Linear(hidden_dim, 1, bias=False)

    def forward(self, decoder_state, encoder_outputs):
        # decoder_state: [batch, hidden]  encoder_outputs: [batch, n, hidden]
        n = encoder_outputs.size(1)
        query = decoder_state.unsqueeze(1).repeat(1, n, 1)
        energy = torch.tanh(self.attn(torch.cat([query, encoder_outputs], dim=-1)))
        scores = self.v(energy).squeeze(-1)          # [batch, n]
        alpha = F.softmax(scores, dim=-1)             # step 2: normalize
        context = torch.bmm(alpha.unsqueeze(1), encoder_outputs).squeeze(1)  # step 3
        return context, alpha


class AttentionDecoderStep(nn.Module):
    def __init__(self, vocab_size, emb_dim, hidden_dim):
        super().__init__()
        self.embed = nn.Embedding(vocab_size, emb_dim)
        self.rnn_cell = nn.LSTMCell(emb_dim + hidden_dim, hidden_dim)
        self.attention = BahdanauAttention(hidden_dim)
        self.output_layer = nn.Linear(hidden_dim * 2, vocab_size)

    def forward(self, y_prev, h_prev, c_prev, encoder_outputs):
        context, alpha = self.attention(h_prev, encoder_outputs)
        rnn_input = torch.cat([self.embed(y_prev), context], dim=-1)
        h_t, c_t = self.rnn_cell(rnn_input, (h_prev, c_prev))
        logits = self.output_layer(torch.cat([h_t, context], dim=-1))
        return logits, h_t, c_t, alpha
```

## Common Mistakes

- **Thinking attention was invented for Transformers.** It was introduced specifically to fix the Seq2Seq context bottleneck years before "Attention Is All You Need" — the Transformer's contribution was showing that recurrence could be removed *entirely* and self-attention alone was sufficient.
- **Confusing teacher forcing with the deployment behavior.** Training feeds ground-truth previous tokens; inference must feed the model's own (possibly wrong) predictions, a mismatch known as **exposure bias** that can degrade generation quality on long outputs.
- **Assuming attention weights are a perfect alignment.** They're a learned soft weighting that often approximates linguistic alignment but isn't guaranteed to correspond to any ground-truth word alignment.
- **Forgetting that the "context vector" in attention-augmented Seq2Seq is per-decoder-step**, not a single fixed vector — this is precisely the change that fixed the original bottleneck.
