## Intuition

Before 2017, sequence models (RNNs, LSTMs) processed tokens one at a time, carrying information forward through a hidden state. That's inherently sequential — you can't process token 10 before token 9 — which caps parallelism and makes long-range dependencies hard to learn (information has to survive many sequential updates).

The Transformer's core bet: **throw away recurrence entirely** and let every token attend directly to every other token in the sequence, in parallel, via an explicit "which other tokens are relevant to me" mechanism — self-attention. This makes long-range dependencies a single computational hop instead of a chain of sequential updates, and makes the whole sequence parallelizable on a GPU.

<Callout type="tip" title="The one-sentence idea">
  Self-attention computes, for every token, a weighted average of every other token's representation — where the weights are learned relevance scores between token pairs. It's a fully-connected, content-based routing mechanism, in place of the fixed, position-based routing of convolution or recurrence.
</Callout>

## Scaled Dot-Product Attention — The Core Operation

Every token's representation is projected into three vectors: a **Query** ("what am I looking for"), a **Key** ("what do I contain"), and a **Value** ("what do I actually offer if selected"). For a full sequence, stack these as matrices $Q, K, V \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times d_k}$:

$$
\text{Attention}(Q, K, V) = \text{softmax}\!\left( \frac{QK^\top}{\sqrt{d_k}} \right) V
$$

Step by step:

1. $QK^\top$ computes a raw relevance score between every pair of tokens — token $i$'s query dotted with token $j$'s key — giving an $n \times n$ score matrix.
2. Dividing by $\sqrt{d_k}$ (the **scaling** in "scaled dot-product") prevents the dot products from growing large in magnitude as $d_k$ increases, which would push softmax into regions with vanishing gradients. This single detail is why the mechanism trains stably at scale.
3. `softmax` (applied per row) turns raw scores into a probability distribution over "which tokens to attend to."
4. Multiplying by $V$ produces, for each token, a weighted average of every other token's value vector — weighted exactly by how relevant that other token was judged to be.

## Multi-Head Attention

A single attention computation forces all relevance information through one $d_k$-dimensional bottleneck. Multi-head attention runs $h$ independent attention computations in parallel, each with its own learned projections, then concatenates and mixes the results:

$$
\text{MultiHead}(Q, K, V) = \text{Concat}(\text{head}_1, \ldots, \text{head}_h) \, W^O
$$
$$
\text{head}_i = \text{Attention}(Q W_i^Q,\ K W_i^K,\ V W_i^V)
$$

Each head can specialize — empirically, different heads in trained Transformers learn to track different relationships (e.g., one head tracks syntactic dependencies, another tracks coreference). This is analogous to how multiple convolutional filters each learn different feature detectors.

Two design details matter enormously in practice:

- **Residual connections** (the `Add` steps) let gradients flow directly through the network via the identity path, which is what makes stacking dozens of Transformer blocks trainable at all — without them, deep stacks suffer the same vanishing-gradient problems as deep MLPs (see [Backpropagation Derivation](/docs/foundations/calculus-optimization/backpropagation-derivation)).
- **Layer normalization** stabilizes the distribution of activations at each layer, independent of batch statistics (unlike batch norm), which matters for variable-length sequences and small batch sizes.

The feed-forward block applies the *same* two-layer MLP independently to every token position — it doesn't mix information across positions (only attention does that). Conceptually: attention moves information *between* tokens; the feed-forward layer processes information *within* each token.

## Positional Encoding

Self-attention itself is **permutation-invariant** — shuffle the input tokens and $QK^\top$ gives the same set of pairwise scores, just reindexed. Without an explicit position signal, "the cat sat on the mat" and "mat the sat cat on the" would look identical. The original paper injects sinusoidal positional encodings additively into the input embeddings:

$$
PE_{(pos, 2i)} = \sin\!\left(\frac{pos}{10000^{2i/d_{\text{model}}}}\right), \qquad PE_{(pos, 2i+1)} = \cos\!\left(\frac{pos}{10000^{2i/d_{\text{model}}}}\right)
$$

Modern LLMs mostly replace this with **RoPE** (Rotary Position Embedding), which encodes position by rotating query/key vectors rather than adding a fixed vector — see [Positional Encoding & RoPE](/docs/llms/tokenization-representations/positional-encoding-rope) for the full derivation and why it generalizes better to long contexts.

## Encoder-Decoder vs. Decoder-Only

The original Transformer was an encoder-decoder (for machine translation): the encoder processes the source sequence bidirectionally, the decoder generates the target sequence autoregressively while cross-attending to the encoder's output. Modern LLMs (the GPT family) are **decoder-only**: a single stack where every token can only attend to earlier tokens (enforced via a **causal mask** — setting future positions' attention scores to $-\infty$ before softmax), trained to predict the next token.

## Complexity — The Central Bottleneck

Self-attention's defining cost is computing the full $n \times n$ score matrix:

| Operation | Time | Memory |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Self-attention (full) | $O(n^2 \cdot d)$ | $O(n^2)$ for the attention matrix |
| Recurrent layer (RNN/LSTM) | $O(n \cdot d^2)$ | $O(d)$, but $O(n)$ *sequential* steps — not parallelizable |
| Feed-forward layer | $O(n \cdot d^2)$ | $O(n \cdot d)$ |

The quadratic-in-sequence-length cost of attention is precisely why long-context serving needs techniques like [FlashAttention](/docs/llms/inference-serving/flash-attention) (roadmap, avoids materializing the full $n \times n$ matrix) and why sub-quadratic architectures like [State Space Models](/docs/deep-learning/modern-architectures/state-space-models) (roadmap, e.g. Mamba) exist as alternatives for very long sequences.
