## Intuition

Instead of writing down an explicit likelihood for the data (as a VAE does with its decoder distribution), a **GAN** trains a generator implicitly, by pitting it against an adversary. A **generator** $G$ tries to produce fake samples indistinguishable from real data; a **discriminator** $D$ tries to tell real samples from $G$'s fakes. Train both simultaneously, and — if the game reaches its intended equilibrium — $G$ ends up producing samples the discriminator can't distinguish from real data at all.

This is a **counterfeiter vs. detective** setup: the counterfeiter ($G$) never sees real currency directly, only feedback from the detective ($D$) about whether its latest forgery got caught. As the detective gets better, the counterfeiter is forced to improve; as the counterfeiter gets better, the detective is forced to sharpen its detection. Neither network needs an explicit density model of the data — the discriminator's gradient is the only signal $G$ ever receives.

<Callout type="tip" title="The one-sentence idea">
  Train a generator and a discriminator against each other in a minimax game — the generator to fool the discriminator, the discriminator to catch the generator — until the generator's output distribution matches the real data distribution closely enough that the discriminator can do no better than a coin flip.
</Callout>

## The Minimax Objective

Let $p_{\text{data}}$ be the true data distribution, $p_z$ a simple prior over a latent noise vector (e.g., $\mathcal{N}(0, I)$), $G$ a generator mapping noise to data space, and $D$ a discriminator mapping data to a scalar in $(0,1)$ interpreted as "probability this input is real." The original GAN objective is:

$$
\min_G \max_D \; V(D, G) = \mathbb{E}_{x \sim p_{\text{data}}}\big[\log D(x)\big] + \mathbb{E}_{z \sim p_z}\big[\log\big(1 - D(G(z))\big)\big]
$$

Read this from the inside out:

- $D$ wants to **maximize** $V$: assign $D(x) \to 1$ for real data (maximizing $\log D(x)$) and $D(G(z)) \to 0$ for generated data (maximizing $\log(1 - D(G(z)))$). This is exactly the binary cross-entropy loss for a real-vs-fake classifier.
- $G$ wants to **minimize** $V$: it only controls the second term, and minimizing $\mathbb{E}_z[\log(1 - D(G(z)))]$ means pushing $D(G(z)) \to 1$ — fooling the discriminator into classifying its fakes as real.

**Why this is a well-defined game with a meaningful solution.** For a *fixed* $G$, the optimal discriminator has a closed form: $D^*(x) = \dfrac{p_{\text{data}}(x)}{p_{\text{data}}(x) + p_g(x)}$, where $p_g$ is the distribution induced by $G$ (derivable by taking the functional derivative of $V$ with respect to $D(x)$ pointwise and setting it to zero). Substituting $D^*$ back into $V$ and simplifying shows that, for the optimal discriminator, minimizing over $G$ is equivalent to minimizing the **Jensen-Shannon divergence** between $p_{\text{data}}$ and $p_g$ (up to an additive constant):

$$
V(D^*, G) = -\log 4 + 2 \cdot D_{JS}(p_{\text{data}} \,\|\, p_g)
$$

Since $D_{JS} \geq 0$ with equality iff the two distributions are identical, the global minimum of the game over $G$ (given an optimal $D$ at every step) is achieved exactly when $p_g = p_{\text{data}}$ — the generator's distribution matches the data distribution, and the optimal discriminator is $D^*(x) = \tfrac{1}{2}$ everywhere (it can do no better than chance).

## Training Loop

In practice, $D$ and $G$ are updated in alternation, not solved to convergence at each step (that closed-form $D^*$ assumes $G$ is fixed, which never truly holds during training):

1. Sample a minibatch of real data $x \sim p_{\text{data}}$ and noise $z \sim p_z$; generate fakes $G(z)$.
2. Update $D$ by ascending its gradient on $V(D,G)$ (a standard binary classification loss: real=1, fake=0), holding $G$ fixed.
3. Sample fresh noise $z \sim p_z$; update $G$ by descending its gradient on $V(D,G)$ (equivalently, ascending $D(G(z))$), holding $D$ fixed.
4. Repeat — the two networks chase a moving target defined by each other's most recent update.

## Instability Issues

The minimax formulation is elegant on paper but notoriously hard to optimize in practice, for two well-documented reasons:

**1. Vanishing gradients when $D$ is too strong.** Early in training, $G$'s samples are easy to distinguish from real data, so $D$ quickly learns to output $D(G(z)) \approx 0$ confidently. But $G$'s original loss, $\log(1 - D(G(z)))$, has a **near-zero gradient with respect to $G$'s parameters exactly when $D(G(z)) \approx 0$** — the function $\log(1-y)$ is nearly flat near $y=0$. So the generator gets almost no learning signal precisely when it needs it most (when it's still bad).

**2. Mode collapse.** $G$ can achieve a low loss by producing only a small, high-quality subset of the true data distribution's modes (e.g., generating only one or two distinct digit styles instead of the full variety) if that subset reliably fools the current $D$. Because $G$'s only objective is to fool $D$ — not to cover the full data distribution — nothing in the vanilla objective directly penalizes collapsing onto a few modes, and the alternating optimization can oscillate between different narrow modes without ever converging to full coverage.

**The non-saturating loss fix.** The original GAN paper itself proposes a practical fix for the vanishing-gradient issue: instead of having $G$ minimize $\log(1 - D(G(z)))$, have it **maximize $\log D(G(z))$** directly:

$$
\max_G \; \mathbb{E}_{z \sim p_z}\big[\log D(G(z))\big]
$$

This objective has the same fixed point (both are optimized when $D(G(z)) \to 1$) but a much steeper gradient when $D(G(z))$ is small — $\log(y)$ has a large derivative near $y=0$, exactly where $\log(1-y)$'s derivative vanishes. This "non-saturating" loss is what's actually used in most practical GAN implementations, even though the original minimax derivation is stated in terms of the saturating form.

Mode collapse has no single universally accepted fix within the vanilla framework; mitigations include minibatch discrimination (letting $D$ compare samples within a batch to detect low diversity), unrolled optimization, and architectural/objective changes in later GAN variants (e.g., Wasserstein GAN's use of the Earth Mover's distance, which provides a smoother, more informative training signal than Jensen-Shannon divergence in regions where the two distributions have little overlap).

## Implementation Sketch

```python
import torch
import torch.nn as nn

# --- Training step (non-saturating generator loss) ---
def train_step(G, D, opt_G, opt_D, real_batch, z_dim, device):
    bce = nn.BCELoss()
    batch_size = real_batch.size(0)
    real_labels = torch.ones(batch_size, 1, device=device)
    fake_labels = torch.zeros(batch_size, 1, device=device)

    # 1) Update D: classify real as 1, fake (detached) as 0
    z = torch.randn(batch_size, z_dim, device=device)
    fake_batch = G(z)
    d_loss = bce(D(real_batch), real_labels) + bce(D(fake_batch.detach()), fake_labels)
    opt_D.zero_grad(); d_loss.backward(); opt_D.step()

    # 2) Update G: non-saturating loss -- maximize log D(G(z)), i.e. minimize -log D(G(z))
    z = torch.randn(batch_size, z_dim, device=device)
    fake_batch = G(z)
    g_loss = bce(D(fake_batch), real_labels)   # label flip is the "fool the discriminator" trick
    opt_G.zero_grad(); g_loss.backward(); opt_G.step()

    return d_loss.item(), g_loss.item()
```

Note the label-flip trick in step 2: computing binary cross-entropy against `real_labels` for the generator's fakes is algebraically the same as maximizing $\log D(G(z))$ — the standard way this non-saturating loss is implemented.

## Common Mistakes

<ProsCons
  pros={[
    "Produces sharp, high-frequency-detail samples, generally sharper than vanilla VAE reconstructions for image data.",
    "No explicit likelihood computation needed — useful when the true data distribution has no convenient tractable density form.",
    "The adversarial signal from D can capture subtle realism cues that a fixed pixel-wise loss (as in VAEs) would average away.",
  ]}
  cons={[
    "Training is a two-player game with no guaranteed convergence in practice — oscillation and instability are common, unlike a VAE's single well-posed optimization.",
    "Mode collapse can silently reduce sample diversity while the loss curves look reasonable.",
    "No explicit likelihood estimate is available, making quantitative comparison across models and detection of overfitting harder than for likelihood-based models.",
    "Very sensitive to hyperparameters and the relative capacity/learning rate balance between G and D -- an imbalance in either direction (too-strong D or too-strong G) degrades training.",
  ]}
/>

A common practical mistake: using the saturating loss $\log(1-D(G(z)))$ as written in the original minimax formula for the generator's actual gradient step, rather than the non-saturating $\log D(G(z))$ form — this reliably stalls generator learning once the discriminator becomes confident.
