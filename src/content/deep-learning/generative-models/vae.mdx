## Intuition

A generative model tries to learn $p(x)$ — the distribution the training data was drawn from — well enough to sample new, plausible data from it. The natural way to build this is to posit a simple latent variable $z$ (say, a standard Gaussian) and a decoder network $p_\theta(x \mid z)$ that maps latents to data. Then $p(x) = \int p_\theta(x \mid z) p(z)\, dz$.

The problem is that integral is intractable for any decoder expressive enough to matter — you cannot enumerate every $z$ that could plausibly have produced a given $x$. A **Variational Autoencoder (VAE)** sidesteps this by learning a second network, an **encoder** $q_\phi(z \mid x)$, that approximates the true (also intractable) posterior $p_\theta(z \mid x)$. Training both networks jointly to maximize a tractable *lower bound* on $\log p(x)$ — rather than $\log p(x)$ itself — is the entire trick.

<Callout type="tip" title="The one-sentence idea">
  Instead of maximizing the intractable log-likelihood directly, a VAE maximizes a tractable lower bound on it (the ELBO), which decomposes into "reconstruct the input well" plus "keep the encoder's latent distribution close to a simple prior" — and a clever reparameterization keeps the whole thing differentiable despite the sampling step in the middle.
</Callout>

## Deriving the ELBO from Jensen's Inequality

Start from the quantity we actually want to maximize: the log marginal likelihood of the data, $\log p_\theta(x)$. Introduce the approximate posterior $q_\phi(z \mid x)$ by multiplying and dividing inside the integral (a standard trick):

$$
\log p_\theta(x) = \log \int p_\theta(x, z)\, dz = \log \int q_\phi(z \mid x) \frac{p_\theta(x, z)}{q_\phi(z \mid x)}\, dz = \log \, \mathbb{E}_{q_\phi(z \mid x)}\!\left[\frac{p_\theta(x, z)}{q_\phi(z \mid x)}\right]
$$

Now apply **Jensen's inequality**. Because $\log$ is a concave function, $\log \mathbb{E}[Y] \geq \mathbb{E}[\log Y]$ for any random variable $Y$. Applying this with $Y = p_\theta(x,z) / q_\phi(z \mid x)$:

$$
\log p_\theta(x) \geq \mathbb{E}_{q_\phi(z \mid x)}\!\left[\log \frac{p_\theta(x, z)}{q_\phi(z \mid x)}\right] \triangleq \mathcal{L}(\theta, \phi; x)
$$

This lower bound $\mathcal{L}$ is the **Evidence Lower BOund (ELBO)** — "evidence" being another name for $\log p_\theta(x)$. Because it's a lower bound, maximizing it pushes $\log p_\theta(x)$ up too (though not necessarily to its maximum — the bound has slack, discussed below).

**Decomposing the ELBO.** Expand $p_\theta(x,z) = p_\theta(x \mid z)\, p(z)$ and split the expectation:

$$
\mathcal{L}(\theta, \phi; x) = \underbrace{\mathbb{E}_{q_\phi(z\mid x)}\big[\log p_\theta(x \mid z)\big]}_{\text{reconstruction term}} \;-\; \underbrace{D_{KL}\big(q_\phi(z \mid x)\,\|\,p(z)\big)}_{\text{KL-to-prior term}}
$$

**How much slack is in the bound?** It's exact: $\log p_\theta(x) - \mathcal{L}(\theta,\phi;x) = D_{KL}\big(q_\phi(z\mid x) \,\|\, p_\theta(z \mid x)\big) \geq 0$. So the gap between the true log-likelihood and the ELBO is precisely the KL divergence between the approximate posterior and the true (intractable) posterior — the better $q_\phi$ approximates $p_\theta(z\mid x)$, the tighter the bound, and maximizing the ELBO over $\phi$ simultaneously tightens the bound and improves the approximate posterior.

## What the Two Terms Enforce

- **Reconstruction term** $\mathbb{E}_{q_\phi(z\mid x)}[\log p_\theta(x\mid z)]$: sample a latent $z$ from the encoder's distribution for input $x$, then ask the decoder to assign high likelihood to reconstructing $x$ from that $z$. In practice this is implemented as a reconstruction loss (mean-squared error for Gaussian decoders, cross-entropy for Bernoulli/categorical decoders). It pulls the model toward faithfully encoding-then-decoding each training point.
- **KL-to-prior term** $D_{KL}(q_\phi(z\mid x) \| p(z))$: penalizes the encoder's output distribution for straying from the prior $p(z)$ (typically $\mathcal{N}(0, I)$). This is what makes the latent space usable for *generation* — without it, the encoder could map each $x$ to an arbitrary, disjoint region of latent space (perfect reconstruction, useless prior), and sampling $z \sim p(z)$ at generation time would land in regions the decoder never saw during training. The KL term forces every input's encoded distribution to overlap with — and collectively tile — the same prior region, so sampling from the prior at generation time produces latents the decoder has actually learned to decode.

These two terms are in direct tension: perfect reconstruction wants a unique, spread-out code per input (minimizing overlap, hence information loss); the KL term wants every code compressed toward the same prior. The balance between them is literally what makes the latent space both *informative* (reconstruction) and *generative* (samplable from the prior).

## The Reparameterization Trick

The encoder outputs the parameters of a distribution, typically $q_\phi(z \mid x) = \mathcal{N}(\mu_\phi(x), \sigma_\phi^2(x))$, and training requires sampling $z \sim q_\phi(z \mid x)$ to evaluate the reconstruction term, then backpropagating gradients of the loss with respect to $\phi$ (the encoder's parameters, which produced $\mu$ and $\sigma$).

**Why direct sampling breaks differentiation:** the sampling operation $z \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu, \sigma^2)$ is a stochastic node in the computation graph. There is no way to write $\partial z / \partial \mu$ or $\partial z / \partial \sigma$ for a sample drawn from a distribution parameterized by $\mu, \sigma$ — the randomness is injected in a way that has no defined derivative with respect to the parameters that generated it. Autodiff has nothing to differentiate through.

**The fix:** rewrite the sampling as a deterministic, differentiable function of the parameters plus an *independent* noise source:

$$
z = \mu_\phi(x) + \sigma_\phi(x) \odot \epsilon, \qquad \epsilon \sim \mathcal{N}(0, I)
$$

Sampling $\epsilon$ from a fixed, parameter-free standard normal is still stochastic, but it no longer depends on $\phi$ — so it can be treated as an external input, like a data augmentation. Given a sampled $\epsilon$, $z$ is now an ordinary differentiable function of $\mu_\phi(x)$ and $\sigma_\phi(x)$: $\partial z/\partial \mu = 1$ and $\partial z/\partial \sigma = \epsilon$, both well-defined. Gradients now flow from the reconstruction loss, through $z$, into $\mu_\phi$ and $\sigma_\phi$, and into every upstream encoder weight — exactly like a normal feedforward layer, just with one extra additive noise input.

This is the whole trick: move the source of randomness *outside* the parameterized part of the graph so the parameterized part is a deterministic, differentiable transformation.

## Implementation Sketch

```python
import torch
import torch.nn as nn
import torch.nn.functional as F

class VAE(nn.Module):
    def __init__(self, input_dim, hidden_dim, latent_dim):
        super().__init__()
        self.enc = nn.Sequential(nn.Linear(input_dim, hidden_dim), nn.ReLU())
        self.to_mu = nn.Linear(hidden_dim, latent_dim)
        self.to_logvar = nn.Linear(hidden_dim, latent_dim)
        self.dec = nn.Sequential(
            nn.Linear(latent_dim, hidden_dim), nn.ReLU(),
            nn.Linear(hidden_dim, input_dim), nn.Sigmoid(),
        )

    def encode(self, x):
        h = self.enc(x)
        return self.to_mu(h), self.to_logvar(h)

    def reparameterize(self, mu, logvar):
        std = torch.exp(0.5 * logvar)
        eps = torch.randn_like(std)          # noise sampled OUTSIDE the parameterized path
        return mu + std * eps                # differentiable w.r.t. mu, logvar

    def forward(self, x):
        mu, logvar = self.encode(x)
        z = self.reparameterize(mu, logvar)
        x_hat = self.dec(z)
        return x_hat, mu, logvar

def vae_loss(x_hat, x, mu, logvar):
    recon = F.binary_cross_entropy(x_hat, x, reduction="sum")
    # closed-form KL between N(mu, sigma^2) and N(0, I)
    kl = -0.5 * torch.sum(1 + logvar - mu.pow(2) - logvar.exp())
    return recon + kl
```

The KL term against a standard normal prior has a closed form (no sampling needed to estimate it), which is one reason the Gaussian prior/posterior pairing is the default choice — it makes that half of the loss exact and cheap.

## Common Mistakes

<ProsCons
  pros={[
    "Explicit, tractable training objective (no adversarial game, no min-max instability) — training is a standard gradient-descent optimization.",
    "Produces a smooth, structured latent space that supports meaningful interpolation between points and, often, disentangled attributes.",
    "Provides an explicit (lower-bound) estimate of data likelihood, useful for anomaly detection and model comparison.",
  ]}
  cons={[
    "Reconstructions are often blurrier than GAN or diffusion samples — commonly attributed to the pixel-wise reconstruction loss averaging over plausible outputs, plus the bound's looseness when q_phi is a poor approximation.",
    "Choosing the wrong prior/posterior family (e.g., a unimodal Gaussian posterior for a genuinely multimodal true posterior) limits how tight the ELBO can get.",
    "The KL term can dominate early in training and collapse the latent code to the prior (\"posterior collapse\"), especially with a powerful decoder that can ignore z — a known failure mode requiring techniques like KL annealing.",
  ]}
/>

Another frequent mistake: forgetting that the reparameterization trick requires the noise source to be independent of the parameters being optimized — sampling $\epsilon$ from a distribution that itself depends on $\phi$ would reintroduce the exact non-differentiability the trick is meant to remove.
