## The Big Shift: From "One Best Model" to Specialization

Earlier LLM eras had a fairly clear "best model" at any given time. As of mid-2026, the frontier has fragmented: different labs' flagship models lead on different axes — coding, structured reasoning, abstract/scientific reasoning, multimodal input, or cost-efficiency — and no single model dominates every benchmark simultaneously. Choosing a model is now a genuine trade-off analysis, not a lookup.

<Callout type="note" title="Reading MoE parameter counts">
  For Mixture-of-Experts models, "total parameters" and "active parameters per token" are both meaningful and very different numbers — a 1T-parameter MoE model might only activate ~30–40B parameters for any given token, which is what actually determines per-token inference cost and latency. Always check which number a benchmark or cost comparison is quoting. See [Mixture of Experts](/docs/deep-learning/modern-architectures/mixture-of-experts) (roadmap).
</Callout>

## How to Actually Choose a Model

Benchmark leaderboards answer "what scores highest," not "what's right for this system." A more durable framework:

<KeyTakeaways items={[
  "Match the axis to the job: coding-heavy agents should weight SWE-bench-style and real-repo evals heavily; research/analysis tools should weight reasoning and long-context comprehension; customer-facing chat should weight instruction-following and tone.",
  "Cost-per-token differences between top proprietary and top open-weight models can be an order of magnitude or more — for high-volume workloads, this often outweighs a few benchmark points of quality difference.",
  "Context window size and effective long-context recall are not the same thing — a model can advertise a huge context window while still degrading in accuracy on information buried in the middle of it ('lost in the middle').",
  "Licensing matters operationally as much as capability does: MIT/Apache-licensed open-weight models permit self-hosting and fine-tuning in ways closed API-only models don't.",
  "Benchmarks saturate and get gamed over time (via train-set contamination or benchmark-specific tuning) — cross-check any single benchmark claim against multiple independent evaluations before trusting it.",
]} />
