## Intuition

SFT teaches a model to produce well-formatted, on-topic responses, but it can't teach the model *which of two reasonable responses is better* — SFT's training data is just a set of target responses, with no notion of "better" or "worse" built in. Humans, on the other hand, are much better at *comparing* two responses than at writing a perfect one from scratch — ranking is an easier, more reliable annotation task than authorship.

Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF) is built around that observation. Rather than trying to collect a dataset of "ideal" responses (which is what SFT does), it collects **comparisons**: for a given prompt, which of two candidate responses do humans prefer? Those comparisons train a separate model — the **reward model** — to predict human preference as a scalar score, and that scalar score becomes the reward signal for reinforcement learning.

<Callout type="tip" title="The one-sentence idea">
  RLHF turns pairwise human preferences into a learned scalar reward function, then uses reinforcement learning (PPO) to push the language model's outputs toward whatever the reward model scores highly — while a KL penalty against the original SFT model keeps the policy from drifting so far that it starts exploiting quirks in the reward model instead of genuinely improving.
</Callout>

## Stage 1: Start From an SFT Model

RLHF never starts from a raw pretrained base model — it starts from a model that has already been through supervised fine-tuning (see the SFT page). This SFT model, denoted $\pi^{\text{SFT}}$, serves two roles going forward: it's the initialization for the RL policy, *and* it's the fixed reference point the KL penalty in Stage 3 measures distance against.

## Stage 2: Train a Reward Model on Pairwise Preferences

For a given prompt $x$, generate multiple candidate responses from the SFT model, and have human labelers pick which of a pair they prefer — producing a dataset of triples $(x, y_w, y_l)$ where $y_w$ ("winner") is the preferred response and $y_l$ ("loser") is the rejected one.

The reward model $r_\phi(x, y)$ is trained to assign a higher scalar score to the preferred response, using a **Bradley-Terry** model of pairwise comparison — a standard statistical model for "probability that A beats B" derived from underlying scalar scores:

$$
p(y_w \succ y_l \mid x) = \sigma\big(r_\phi(x, y_w) - r_\phi(x, y_l)\big)
$$

where $\sigma$ is the logistic sigmoid. The reward model is trained by maximizing the log-likelihood of the observed human preferences under this model — equivalently, minimizing:

$$
\mathcal{L}_{\text{RM}}(\phi) = -\mathbb{E}_{(x, y_w, y_l)}\Big[\log \sigma\big(r_\phi(x, y_w) - r_\phi(x, y_l)\big)\Big]
$$

Architecturally, $r_\phi$ is typically the SFT model itself with its unembedding (softmax output) layer replaced by a single scalar-output head, then fine-tuned on this preference loss. It is a completely separate model from the policy that will later be optimized — the reward model is trained once (or periodically refreshed) and then held fixed during the RL stage.

## Stage 3: RL Fine-Tuning Against the Reward Model, With a KL Penalty

With a trained reward model in hand, the SFT model is now further optimized via reinforcement learning — treating response generation as a sequence of actions, and the reward model's score on the completed response as the reward. The policy $\pi_\theta$ (initialized from $\pi^{\text{SFT}}$) is optimized to maximize:

$$
\mathbb{E}_{x \sim \mathcal{D},\, y \sim \pi_\theta(\cdot \mid x)}\Big[r_\phi(x, y) - \beta \, D_{\text{KL}}\big(\pi_\theta(\cdot \mid x) \,\|\, \pi^{\text{SFT}}(\cdot \mid x)\big)\Big]
$$

The reward term alone would push the policy purely toward whatever maximizes the reward model's score — but the reward model is an imperfect proxy for actual human preference, and a policy free to move arbitrarily far from the SFT model can find "reward hacks": outputs that score highly under $r_\phi$'s imperfect approximation without actually being good responses (degenerate repetition, exploiting scoring quirks, sycophantic phrasing the reward model over-values, and so on). The **KL penalty term**, weighted by $\beta$, directly penalizes the policy for diverging from the SFT model's distribution — it's a leash, not a hard constraint, letting the policy move toward higher reward while limiting how far and how fast it can drift from a distribution known to produce coherent, on-distribution text.

**PPO (Proximal Policy Optimization)** is the specific RL algorithm used to perform this optimization. It's chosen primarily because it constrains how much the policy can change per update step (via a clipped surrogate objective), which keeps training stable — a critical property here since a policy that's allowed to swing wildly between updates could ruin the delicate balance between reward-seeking and staying close to the SFT model. See [PPO & Trust Region Methods](/docs/machine-learning/reinforcement-learning/ppo-trust-region) for the full derivation of PPO's clipped objective and why trust-region constraints matter for policy gradient stability.

## Why the KL Term Is Load-Bearing, Not Optional

Removing the KL penalty (setting $\beta = 0$) turns the objective into pure reward maximization, and because $r_\phi$ is a learned approximation trained on a finite preference dataset, it inevitably has blind spots and exploitable regions the policy can find through unconstrained optimization — this failure mode is called **reward hacking** or **reward over-optimization**. Practically, teams monitor the KL divergence during training as a proxy signal: if it grows too large, the policy is likely drifting into a regime the reward model was never trained to judge reliably, even if the measured reward keeps climbing.

## Code Sketch: RLHF Loop (TRL-style Pseudocode)

```python
# Stage 2: Reward model training
import torch.nn.functional as F

def reward_model_loss(reward_model, prompt, chosen, rejected):
    r_chosen = reward_model(prompt, chosen)     # scalar score
    r_rejected = reward_model(prompt, rejected) # scalar score
    # Bradley-Terry pairwise loss
    return -F.logsigmoid(r_chosen - r_rejected).mean()

# Stage 3: PPO fine-tuning against the frozen reward model
from trl import PPOTrainer, PPOConfig

ppo_config = PPOConfig(model_name="sft-model", learning_rate=1.4e-5, kl_penalty="kl", init_kl_coef=0.2)
ppo_trainer = PPOTrainer(config=ppo_config, model=policy_model, ref_model=sft_reference_model, tokenizer=tokenizer)

for batch in prompt_dataloader:
    query_tensors = batch["input_ids"]
    response_tensors = ppo_trainer.generate(query_tensors)          # sample from current policy
    rewards = [reward_model(q, r) for q, r in zip(query_tensors, response_tensors)]
    # PPOTrainer.step internally: computes advantages, applies the clipped
    # PPO objective, AND subtracts β * KL(π_θ || π_SFT) from the reward per-token
    stats = ppo_trainer.step(query_tensors, response_tensors, rewards)
```
