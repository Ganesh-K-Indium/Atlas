## Intuition

RLHF works, but it's operationally heavy: train a separate reward model, then run an online RL loop (PPO) that repeatedly samples from the policy, scores those samples with the reward model, and updates the policy — with all the variance, instability, and hyperparameter sensitivity RL is known for.

Direct Preference Optimization (Rafailov et al., 2023) asks a sharper question: RLHF's whole pipeline exists to turn preference data into a policy update. Is there a way to go directly from preference data to a policy update, skipping the reward model and the RL loop as separate objects entirely? DPO's answer is yes — and the reason it's possible isn't a heuristic simplification, it's an exact algebraic reparameterization of the same underlying objective RLHF optimizes.

<Callout type="tip" title="The one-sentence idea">
  The optimal policy under RLHF's KL-constrained reward-maximization objective has a closed form that expresses the reward function directly in terms of the policy's own log-probability ratio to the reference model — substituting that closed form into the Bradley-Terry preference loss produces a loss over policy parameters alone, with the reward model and the RL loop eliminated by algebra rather than by approximation.
</Callout>

## Step 1: The Closed-Form Optimal Policy Under RLHF's Objective

Recall RLHF's Stage 3 objective (see the [RLHF](/docs/llms/training-alignment/rlhf) page): maximize expected reward, penalized by KL divergence from a reference policy $\pi_{\text{ref}}$ (the SFT model):

$$
\max_{\pi} \; \mathbb{E}_{x \sim \mathcal{D},\, y \sim \pi(\cdot\mid x)}\big[r(x, y)\big] - \beta\, D_{\text{KL}}\big(\pi(\cdot \mid x) \,\|\, \pi_{\text{ref}}(\cdot \mid x)\big)
$$

This exact objective — KL-regularized reward maximization — has a known closed-form solution (a standard result predating DPO, used in prior RL-as-inference and KL-control literature). For any reward function $r$, the optimal policy is:

$$
\pi_r(y \mid x) = \frac{1}{Z(x)} \, \pi_{\text{ref}}(y \mid x) \, \exp\!\left(\frac{1}{\beta} r(x, y)\right)
$$

where $Z(x) = \sum_y \pi_{\text{ref}}(y \mid x) \exp\left(\frac{1}{\beta} r(x,y)\right)$ is a partition function that normalizes the distribution over all possible responses $y$ — it depends on the prompt $x$ and on $r$, but crucially **not on any specific response** $y$.

## Step 2: Invert the Relationship — Write the Reward in Terms of the Policy

This is DPO's key algebraic move. The closed-form expression above can be rearranged to solve for $r(x, y)$ in terms of the optimal policy $\pi_r$, the reference policy $\pi_{\text{ref}}$, and $Z(x)$:

$$
r(x, y) = \beta \log \frac{\pi_r(y \mid x)}{\pi_{\text{ref}}(y \mid x)} + \beta \log Z(x)
$$

In words: whatever the true reward function is, it is *exactly* recoverable (up to a prompt-dependent constant $\beta \log Z(x)$) from the ratio of the optimal policy to the reference policy. This means a reward function and its induced optimal policy are two sides of the same object — if you already have a candidate policy, you already implicitly have a candidate reward function, without ever needing to fit $r$ separately.

## Step 3: Substitute Into the Bradley-Terry Preference Loss

RLHF's reward model is trained with the Bradley-Terry pairwise preference model:

$$
p^*(y_w \succ y_l \mid x) = \sigma\big(r(x, y_w) - r(x, y_l)\big)
$$

Substitute the Step 2 expression for $r(x,y)$ into this difference:

$$
r(x, y_w) - r(x, y_l) = \beta \log \frac{\pi_r(y_w \mid x)}{\pi_{\text{ref}}(y_w \mid x)} - \beta \log \frac{\pi_r(y_l \mid x)}{\pi_{\text{ref}}(y_l \mid x)} + \underbrace{\beta \log Z(x) - \beta \log Z(x)}_{\text{cancels exactly}}
$$

The $\beta \log Z(x)$ terms are identical for $y_w$ and $y_l$ (both responses share the same prompt $x$, and $Z(x)$ depends only on $x$) — so they **cancel exactly** in the subtraction. This cancellation is what makes the whole trick work: the intractable normalizing constant, which would ordinarily require summing over every possible response, simply disappears from the loss without approximation.

What remains is a preference probability expressed *purely* in terms of the policy's own log-probability ratios to the reference model — no reward model, no partition function:

$$
p^*(y_w \succ y_l \mid x) = \sigma\!\left(\beta \log \frac{\pi_r(y_w \mid x)}{\pi_{\text{ref}}(y_w \mid x)} - \beta \log \frac{\pi_r(y_l \mid x)}{\pi_{\text{ref}}(y_l \mid x)}\right)
$$

## Step 4: The DPO Loss

Replacing the theoretical optimal policy $\pi_r$ with a trainable policy $\pi_\theta$ gives a maximum-likelihood loss directly over $\theta$, trained on the same $(x, y_w, y_l)$ preference triples RLHF's reward model would have used:

$$
\mathcal{L}_{\text{DPO}}(\theta) = -\mathbb{E}_{(x, y_w, y_l) \sim \mathcal{D}}\left[\log \sigma\!\left(\beta \log \frac{\pi_\theta(y_w \mid x)}{\pi_{\text{ref}}(y_w \mid x)} - \beta \log \frac{\pi_\theta(y_l \mid x)}{\pi_{\text{ref}}(y_l \mid x)}\right)\right]
$$

This is a single, static, supervised-style loss — no sampling from the policy during training, no separately trained reward model, no PPO clipping or advantage estimation. $\pi_{\text{ref}}$ is just the frozen SFT model, evaluated (not trained) alongside $\pi_\theta$ to compute the log-ratio.

<Callout type="note" title="What is the 'implicit reward' here?">
  The quantity $\hat r_\theta(x,y) = \beta \log\big(\pi_\theta(y\mid x)/\pi_{\text{ref}}(y\mid x)\big)$ is exactly the reward function DPO is implicitly fitting — it's mathematically the same object as RLHF's $r_\phi(x,y)$, just reparameterized to be a direct function of the policy's own probabilities rather than a separately trained network. DPO doesn't avoid modeling reward; it fuses the reward model and the policy into a single object.
</Callout>

## Why This Is Equivalent to RLHF (Under the Stated Assumptions)

The equivalence claim is precise, not loose: DPO does not approximate RLHF's objective — it optimizes the exact same KL-constrained reward-maximization objective, under the assumption that the Bradley-Terry model correctly describes the preference data. The chain is: (1) the KL-constrained objective has a unique closed-form optimal policy for any reward function, (2) that relationship is invertible, so fitting a policy that satisfies the Bradley-Terry loss under this reparameterization is mathematically the same as fitting a reward model and then solving for its induced optimal policy. DPO simply skips the intermediate reward-model-fitting and RL-optimization steps by directly parameterizing and fitting the *result* of that whole pipeline.

The practical assumptions this rests on: the preference data genuinely follows a Bradley-Terry structure, and $\pi_\theta$ is expressive enough to represent the true optimal policy for some reward function consistent with the data — the same assumptions RLHF's reward-model-plus-RL approach implicitly relies on too.

## Code Sketch (TRL-style Pseudocode)

```python
import torch
import torch.nn.functional as F

def dpo_loss(policy_logps_chosen, policy_logps_rejected,
             ref_logps_chosen, ref_logps_rejected, beta=0.1):
    """
    *_logps_* are sequence-level log p(y|x) — sum of per-token log-probs —
    under the policy π_θ and the frozen reference π_ref respectively.
    """
    policy_logratio = policy_logps_chosen - policy_logps_rejected
    ref_logratio = ref_logps_chosen - ref_logps_rejected

    logits = beta * (policy_logratio - ref_logratio)
    loss = -F.logsigmoid(logits).mean()
    return loss

# Training step sketch
# 1. Forward pass chosen/rejected responses through π_θ (trainable) and π_ref (frozen, no grad)
# 2. Sum per-token log-probs over each response to get sequence log p(y|x)
# 3. Compute dpo_loss and backprop — no sampling, no reward model, no PPO
```

  pros={[
    "Eliminates an entire class of RLHF failure modes tied to online RL — reward hacking against an imperfect reward model, PPO instability, rollout/sampling infrastructure.",
    "Substantially simpler to implement and tune — a single supervised-style loss over a fixed dataset, no reward model or RL loop to maintain.",
    "The equivalence to RLHF is a mathematical result, not an empirical approximation — it holds exactly under the stated Bradley-Terry and expressiveness assumptions.",
  ]}
  cons={[

    "The equivalence argument assumes the policy is expressive enough to represent the true optimal policy for some reward consistent with the data — a real assumption, not a formality, when the base model has structural limitations.",
    "Confusing DPO's implicit reward β·log(π_θ/π_ref) with an interpretable, transferable reward score the way RLHF's explicit reward model provides — DPO's implicit reward is tied to the specific policy and reference model, not a standalone scoring function that generalizes independently.",
