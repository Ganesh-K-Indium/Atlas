## Intuition

The pretraining objective behind essentially every modern LLM is almost embarrassingly simple: given a sequence of tokens, predict the next one. Repeated across trillions of tokens of internet-scale text, this single objective — with no task-specific labels, no human annotation — turns out to force the model to implicitly learn grammar, facts, reasoning patterns, and style, because predicting the next token well requires modeling all of that.

The harder question pretraining raises isn't "what objective to use" (that part is settled) but "given a fixed compute budget, how big should the model be, and how much data should it see?" A bigger model can extract more from the same data; more data can make a smaller model perform better — scaling laws are the empirical answer to how to split a fixed compute budget between the two.

<Callout type="tip" title="The one-sentence idea">
  Pretraining loss falls as a smooth, predictable power law in model size, dataset size, and compute — which means you can extrapolate from small, cheap experiments to decide how large a model to train and how much data to feed it, before ever running the expensive full-scale job.
</Callout>

## The Causal Language Modeling Objective

Given a corpus, the model is trained to maximize the probability it assigns to the actual next token at every position, conditioned only on the tokens that came before it (never tokens that come after — this causal, left-to-right constraint is what makes the objective usable for autoregressive generation at inference time):

$$
\mathcal{L}(\theta) = -\sum_{t=1}^{T} \log p_\theta(x_t \mid x_1, \ldots, x_{t-1})
$$

This is standard cross-entropy loss, applied at every token position in every training sequence, averaged over the whole corpus. Nothing about the objective is task-specific — there's no notion of "question answering" or "summarization" built in. Downstream capability emerges because accurately predicting the next token across a sufficiently broad and large corpus requires implicitly encoding an enormous amount of world knowledge and reasoning structure.

## Scaling Laws: Loss as a Function of Scale

Kaplan et al. (2020) trained several hundred Transformer language models across a wide range of sizes and found that pretraining loss, as a function of model size ($N$, non-embedding parameters), dataset size ($D$, tokens), or compute ($C$), each follows a **power law** when the other two are not the bottleneck:

$$
L(N) \approx \left(\frac{N_c}{N}\right)^{\alpha_N}, \qquad L(D) \approx \left(\frac{D_c}{D}\right)^{\alpha_D}, \qquad L(C) \approx \left(\frac{C_c}{C}\right)^{\alpha_C}
$$

The practically important qualitative findings from that paper:

- Loss decreases **smoothly and predictably** across many orders of magnitude of scale — no evidence of a plateau within the ranges they tested — which is what makes extrapolation from small experiments to large ones a reasonable practice at all.
- Within the regime Kaplan et al. studied, they found it was more compute-efficient to train **very large models on a comparatively modest amount of data and stop before convergence**, rather than training a smaller model to convergence. This specific conclusion — favoring large models trained on relatively little data — is exactly the part later work overturned.

## Chinchilla: The Compute-Optimal Correction

Hoffmann et al. (2022) revisited the same question with a wider and more carefully controlled set of training runs and reached a different practical conclusion: for a fixed compute budget, model size and training-token count should be scaled **at roughly equal rates** — doubling the compute budget means doubling both the model size and the data seen, not disproportionately favoring one over the other. Their headline result, the Chinchilla model, was trained at roughly **20 tokens per parameter** and, at 70B parameters, outperformed the much larger 175B-parameter, ~300B-token GPT-3 (a ratio of under 2 tokens per parameter) despite using similar total training compute — evidence that many earlier large models were significantly **under-trained on data** relative to their parameter count.

$$
N_{\text{opt}}(C) \propto C^{a}, \qquad D_{\text{opt}}(C) \propto C^{b}, \qquad a \approx b \approx 0.5
$$

(Hoffmann et al.'s fitted exponents put $N_{\text{opt}}$ and $D_{\text{opt}}$ growing at very close to the same rate as compute increases — the "roughly equal scaling" conclusion — though the exact fitted constants differ slightly across the three estimation methods used in the paper.)

<Callout type="warning" title="Why the two papers disagreed">
  The discrepancy came primarily from experimental design, not a contradiction in the underlying physics of scaling: Kaplan et al. used a fixed, relatively short warmup/learning-rate schedule across model sizes and didn't always train models to the point their learning-rate schedule intended, which biased their fitted compute-optimal frontier toward favoring parameter count over data. Chinchilla's larger, more carefully controlled sweep (with learning-rate schedules matched to each run's actual token budget) produced the corrected, roughly-equal-scaling conclusion that the field adopted afterward.
</Callout>

## Practical Consequences

Scaling laws turn "how big should I train?" from a guess into an extrapolation problem: run a series of much smaller/cheaper models, fit the power-law curve to their loss, and use the fit to predict the loss (and choose the size/data split) for the full-scale run before committing the budget for it. This is standard practice at major labs precisely because a full pretraining run can cost millions of dollars — nobody wants to discover the wrong size ratio only after the run finishes.

It's worth noting what compute-optimal *doesn't* mean: it minimizes training loss per unit of training compute, but says nothing about inference cost. A model trained well past the "Chinchilla-optimal" token count for its size (often called "overtraining" relative to that ratio) can still be the better choice in practice if it will be served at massive inference volume, since a smaller, more heavily-trained model that reaches similar quality is cheaper to run for the rest of its life — many widely deployed open-weight models are deliberately trained on far more than 20 tokens per parameter for exactly this reason.

## Code Sketch: Fitting a Simple Scaling Law

```python
import numpy as np
from scipy.optimize import curve_fit

# Loss observed for a handful of cheap small-scale training runs
N = np.array([1e7, 3e7, 1e8, 3e8, 1e9])       # non-embedding parameters
loss = np.array([3.7, 3.3, 2.9, 2.6, 2.3])     # measured pretraining loss

def power_law(N, Nc, alpha):
    return (Nc / N) ** alpha

params, _ = curve_fit(power_law, N, loss, p0=[1e12, 0.08])
Nc_fit, alpha_fit = params

# Extrapolate to a much larger, not-yet-trained model size
N_target = 7e10
predicted_loss = power_law(N_target, Nc_fit, alpha_fit)
print(f"Predicted loss at N={N_target:.0e}: {predicted_loss:.3f}")
```

This is a toy version of the real methodology: labs fit these curves on dozens of runs spanning several orders of magnitude, then use the fit to choose the size and data budget for the one expensive run that actually matters.
