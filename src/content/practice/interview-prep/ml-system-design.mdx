## Why ML System Design Is Different

A standard software system design interview asks you to design something that behaves *deterministically* given its inputs — a URL shortener, a rate limiter, a news feed. An ML system design interview adds three axes that don't exist in ordinary system design: the system's core logic is *learned* rather than hand-coded, its quality is *probabilistic and drifts over time*, and there are two entirely separate infrastructures to design — one for training (batch, throughput-oriented) and one for serving (online, latency-oriented) — that must stay consistent with each other.

<Callout type="note" title="What interviewers are actually scoring">
  Nobody expects a fully-specified system in 45 minutes. Interviewers are scoring whether you can (1) turn a vague business goal into a precise, measurable ML problem, (2) reason about real trade-offs (offline vs. online metrics, latency vs. accuracy, freshness vs. stability) with specifics rather than buzzwords, and (3) know what happens *after* the model ships — monitoring, retraining, failure modes.
</Callout>

## A Framework You Can Reuse in Any Interview

Use this seven-step structure as scaffolding. Say the steps out loud as you move through them — it signals structure to the interviewer and keeps you from getting lost in premature detail.

  B --> C["3. Data & features"]
  C --> D["4. Model choice"]
  D --> E["5. Training & serving architecture"]
  E --> F["6. Evaluation: offline + online"]

### 1. Clarify Requirements and Scale

Before touching ML at all, pin down: who is the user, what business metric does this move, what's the traffic volume and latency budget, and what's out of scope. Ask about scale explicitly — a system serving 10,000 requests/day and one serving 1 million requests/second are different systems, not the same system at different sizes. Also clarify the acceptable trade-off between precision and recall, since that shapes everything downstream (see [Metrics: Precision, Recall, F1, ROC-AUC](/docs/machine-learning/evaluation/metrics)).

### 2. Frame the ML Problem

Translate the business goal into a supervised/unsupervised learning problem with a concrete label definition. This is the step candidates skip most often, and it's the one that matters most — a vague goal like "increase engagement" must become something like "predict `P(watch > 30s | user, video, context)` for ranking." State explicitly: what's the label, what's the prediction target, what's the training objective, and how does optimizing that objective actually serve the business goal (and where might it diverge).

### 3. Data and Features

Identify the raw data sources (logs, user profiles, content metadata, real-time signals), how labels are obtained (implicit feedback vs. explicit, and the delay before a label is observed), and the feature categories: user features, item features, contextual features, and cross features. Flag training/serving skew risk early — features computed differently in the offline training pipeline versus the online serving path is one of the most common silent production bugs.

### 4. Model Choice

Pick a specific model family and justify it against the latency budget and data characteristics — don't just say "we'll use deep learning." For structured/tabular signals under a tight latency budget, gradient-boosted trees (see [Gradient Boosting: XGBoost, LightGBM & CatBoost](/docs/machine-learning/supervised-learning/gradient-boosting)) are often the right first model: fast to train, fast to score, strong on tabular features, and easy to debug via feature importance. Deep architectures (two-tower embeddings, transformers) earn their complexity budget when you have large-scale interaction data and the marginal quality lift is worth the added latency and operational cost. State explicitly what you're trading off.

### 5. Training and Serving Architecture

Decide: batch vs. online training, and — separately — batch vs. online (real-time) inference. Most large-scale recommendation and ranking systems use a **multi-stage funnel** (candidate generation → ranking → re-ranking) rather than a single model scoring the entire catalog, because scoring millions of items with an expensive model at request time is infeasible under a tight latency budget. Discuss the serving path concretely: feature store for consistent online/offline features, a model registry, and how the model artifact gets deployed (see [Model Serving](/docs/mlops/production-ml/model-serving) for batch vs. online vs. streaming serving patterns).

### 6. Evaluation: Offline and Online

State the offline metric (AUC, NDCG, log-loss) used during model development, and the online metric (click-through rate, watch time, retention) used in the actual A/B test — and explicitly acknowledge that these can diverge. A model that wins offline on NDCG can lose online because the offline metric doesn't capture position bias, diversity, or downstream effects like content fatigue. This offline/online metric mismatch is one of the most commonly probed failure modes in these interviews (see [Case Studies](/docs/practice/interview-prep/case-studies) for a full diagnostic walkthrough of this exact scenario).

### 7. Monitoring and Iteration

Cover feature drift, prediction drift, label delay, and the retraining cadence. State what alerts you'd set up and what "the model is broken" looks like operationally — a spike in null features, a shift in the prediction distribution, or a drop in coverage (see [Monitoring & Observability for ML](/docs/mlops/production-ml/monitoring-observability)).

## Worked Example: Design a Video Recommendation System (Home Feed)

Let's apply the framework concretely instead of abstractly.

### Step 1 — Requirements and Scale

Goal: recommend videos on the home feed to maximize long-term watch time without sacrificing content diversity or user trust. Scale: 200M daily active users, catalog of 500M videos, feed refreshed on every app open (~2B requests/day), p99 latency budget of 100ms end-to-end for the ranking service. Business constraint: must not systematically starve new/small creators (a "cold-start fairness" requirement) — this shapes candidate generation later.

### Step 2 — Problem Framing

Frame it as pointwise ranking: for a given `(user, candidate video, context)` triple, predict `P(engagement | user, video, context)`, where "engagement" is defined as a weighted combination of watch-time-ratio and an explicit signal (like/share) to avoid pure clickbait optimization. Label: derived from logged impressions — watch-time-ratio is observed with a delay of minutes to hours (a user might watch later), so training data must account for this label latency, typically via a fixed attribution window (e.g., label finalized 24 hours after impression).

### Step 3 — Data and Features

- **User features**: watch history embeddings, subscribed channels, device, time-of-day, session-level signals.
- **Item features**: video embedding (from a separately-trained content model), channel/creator features, video age, historical CTR/watch-time-ratio (with Bayesian smoothing to avoid overweighting videos with tiny impression counts).
- **Cross features**: user-topic affinity computed from watch history, recency-weighted.
- **Skew risk**: historical CTR must be computed identically offline and online (same aggregation window, same smoothing) — a common bug is the online feature store serving a fresher aggregate than what the model was trained on, silently degrading calibration.

### Step 4 — Model Choice

Use a **multi-stage funnel**, since scoring 500M videos per request at 100ms is not feasible:

1. **Candidate generation** (retrieve ~500 candidates from 500M): a two-tower neural retrieval model (user tower + item tower, dot-product scoring) backed by approximate nearest-neighbor search (e.g., HNSW). Two-tower models are chosen here specifically because the item tower can be precomputed and indexed offline, making retrieval sub-millisecond.
2. **Ranking** (score ~500 candidates down to ~50): a gradient-boosted tree model (LightGBM) on the richer feature set described above — chosen over a deep ranker here because tabular cross-features dominate the signal at this stage, GBTs give fast, well-calibrated scores, and feature importance from the trained trees provides straightforward debuggability in production (see [Gradient Boosting](/docs/machine-learning/supervised-learning/gradient-boosting)). A deep ranker is a reasonable alternative if the team wants to jointly learn feature interactions, at the cost of interpretability and serving latency.
3. **Re-ranking** (final 50 → displayed order): a lightweight heuristic/learned layer that enforces diversity (no two consecutive videos from the same channel) and the cold-start-fairness constraint from Step 1 (guaranteed slots for a diversity-sampled set of newer creators).

### Step 5 — Training and Serving Architecture

  subgraph Offline
    Logs[Impression + Engagement Logs] --> FeatEng[Feature Engineering]
    FeatEng --> FS[(Feature Store)]
    FeatEng --> Train[Model Training: Two-Tower + LightGBM Ranker]
    Train --> Registry[(Model Registry)]
  end
  subgraph Online
    Request[User Opens App] --> Retrieval[Candidate Retrieval - ANN Index]
    Retrieval --> Ranker[Ranking Service - LightGBM]
    FS -.serves online features.-> Ranker
    Registry -.deploys model.-> Ranker
    Ranker --> Rerank[Re-ranking: diversity + fairness]
    Rerank --> Feed[Home Feed Response]
    Feed --> Logs
  end
`} />

The feature store is the critical piece that prevents training/serving skew: both the offline training pipeline and the online ranking service read the same feature definitions, computed by the same logic, just at different times (batch vs. real-time). See [Model Serving](/docs/mlops/production-ml/model-serving) for the trade-offs between this real-time serving pattern and simpler batch-precomputed recommendations (which would be cheaper but couldn't react to the current session).

### Step 6 — Evaluation

**Offline**: NDCG@50 and AUC on held-out logged impressions, computed with inverse-propensity weighting to correct for the fact that the logging policy itself biased which videos got shown (avoiding the trap of evaluating a new ranker only on the old ranker's exposure distribution).

**Online**: A/B test measuring total watch time per user per day and a longer-horizon retention metric (7-day return rate), not just session-level CTR — because a model that boosts a single session's watch time via clickbait can quietly erode next-week retention. This is exactly the offline/online mismatch discussed in the framework: a ranker can win on offline NDCG while its online watch-time and retention numbers are flat or negative, usually because the offline evaluation set doesn't capture position bias or long-horizon user fatigue effects.

### Step 7 — Monitoring and Iteration

Track feature drift on the top-20 ranking features (population stability index), prediction-score distribution shift, and a canary comparison between the new model's score distribution and the currently-serving model's, before full rollout. Retrain the ranker daily (fast-moving trend signal) and the two-tower retrieval model weekly (embeddings change more slowly and are more expensive to recompute and re-index). Full detail on drift detection and alerting design lives in [Monitoring & Observability for ML](/docs/mlops/production-ml/monitoring-observability).
