## Intuition

Maximum Likelihood Estimation returns a single number for a parameter and calls it done. But with limited data, that single number is often overconfident — it doesn't express how *uncertain* you should still be. Bayesian statistics treats the unknown parameter itself as a random variable, starting from a **prior** belief about it, and updates that belief into a **posterior** distribution once data arrives — using nothing more than Bayes' theorem, now applied to parameters instead of just events.

<Callout type="tip" title="One-sentence definition">
  Bayesian inference computes $p(\theta \mid \text{data}) \propto p(\text{data} \mid \theta)\, p(\theta)$ — a full distribution over the parameter, not just a point estimate, letting the model express calibrated uncertainty.
</Callout>

This matters practically: a Bayesian neural network can say "I'm not sure" on out-of-distribution inputs; Bayesian optimization (used to tune hyperparameters) needs a posterior over an unknown function to decide where to sample next; and A/B testing with Bayesian methods gives a direct probability that "variant A is better," rather than a p-value that's frequently misinterpreted as one.

## Formal Definition

Given a prior $p(\theta)$ over parameters and a likelihood $p(\mathcal{D} \mid \theta)$ for observed data $\mathcal{D}$, Bayes' theorem gives the **posterior**:

$$
p(\theta \mid \mathcal{D}) = \frac{p(\mathcal{D} \mid \theta)\, p(\theta)}{p(\mathcal{D})}, \qquad p(\mathcal{D}) = \int p(\mathcal{D} \mid \theta)\, p(\theta)\, d\theta
$$

The denominator $p(\mathcal{D})$ (the **marginal likelihood** or **evidence**) doesn't depend on $\theta$ — it's just a normalizing constant — so in practice we work with the proportionality:

$$
p(\theta \mid \mathcal{D}) \propto p(\mathcal{D} \mid \theta)\, p(\theta) \quad \text{(posterior} \propto \text{likelihood} \times \text{prior)}
$$

This proportionality is the entire computational engine of Bayesian inference: multiply likelihood and prior, then normalize (analytically if possible, otherwise via MCMC — see [Markov Chains & Monte Carlo](/docs/foundations/probability-statistics/markov-chains-monte-carlo) — or variational approximation).

## Derivation: The Beta-Bernoulli Conjugate Update

This is the cleanest fully-worked Bayesian update, and it underlies Bayesian A/B testing and Thompson sampling for bandits.

**Setup.** We observe $n$ i.i.d. coin flips $x_1, \ldots, x_n \in \{0,1\}$ with unknown success probability $\theta$. We place a $\text{Beta}(\alpha, \beta)$ prior on $\theta$:

$$
p(\theta) = \frac{\theta^{\alpha-1}(1-\theta)^{\beta-1}}{B(\alpha,\beta)}, \qquad \theta \in [0,1]
$$

where $B(\alpha,\beta)$ is the Beta function (a normalizing constant depending only on $\alpha, \beta$).

**Step 1 — write the likelihood.** With $k = \sum_i x_i$ successes out of $n$ trials:

$$
p(\mathcal{D} \mid \theta) = \theta^k (1-\theta)^{n-k}
$$

**Step 2 — multiply likelihood by prior:**

$$
p(\theta \mid \mathcal{D}) \propto \theta^k(1-\theta)^{n-k} \cdot \theta^{\alpha-1}(1-\theta)^{\beta-1} = \theta^{\alpha+k-1}(1-\theta)^{\beta+n-k-1}
$$

**Step 3 — recognize the form.** This is *exactly* the unnormalized kernel of a $\text{Beta}(\alpha + k,\ \beta + n - k)$ distribution. Since a probability distribution over $[0,1]$ with this kernel is unique (the normalizing constant is whatever makes it integrate to 1), we conclude:

$$
\theta \mid \mathcal{D} \sim \text{Beta}(\alpha + k,\ \beta + n - k)
$$

This is why Beta is called the **conjugate prior** for the Bernoulli/Binomial likelihood: the posterior stays in the same distributional family as the prior, just with updated parameters — no numerical integration required. Intuitively, $\alpha$ and $\beta$ act like "pseudo-counts" of prior successes and failures, and the update simply adds the observed successes/failures to them.

**Posterior mean** (using the known mean formula for Beta): $\mathbb{E}[\theta \mid \mathcal{D}] = \frac{\alpha+k}{\alpha+\beta+n}$ — a weighted average of the prior mean $\frac{\alpha}{\alpha+\beta}$ and the MLE $\frac{k}{n}$, with weights determined by $\alpha+\beta$ (prior "strength") versus $n$ (data size). As $n \to \infty$, this converges to the MLE $k/n$ regardless of the prior — Bayesian and frequentist estimates agree asymptotically.

## Point Estimates from a Posterior: MAP vs. Full Bayesian

If a single point estimate is still needed, the **Maximum A Posteriori (MAP)** estimate is:

$$
\hat\theta_{\text{MAP}} = \arg\max_\theta p(\theta \mid \mathcal{D}) = \arg\max_\theta \left[\log p(\mathcal{D}\mid\theta) + \log p(\theta)\right]
$$

This is literally MLE plus a regularizer — $\log p(\theta)$ acts exactly like the penalty term in L2/L1-regularized regression. A Gaussian prior on weights yields the $\ell_2$ penalty (ridge regression); a Laplace prior yields the $\ell_1$ penalty (LASSO). This is the precise sense in which "regularization is a prior in disguise."

But MAP still collapses the posterior to a point, discarding the uncertainty captured by its spread. **Full Bayesian inference** instead integrates over the entire posterior for downstream tasks — e.g. the posterior predictive distribution for a new point $x^*$:

$$
p(x^* \mid \mathcal{D}) = \int p(x^* \mid \theta)\, p(\theta \mid \mathcal{D})\, d\theta
$$

which averages predictions across all plausible $\theta$ weighted by their posterior probability, rather than betting everything on one $\hat\theta$.

## Code Example

```python
import numpy as np
from scipy import stats

# Bayesian A/B test: two variants, Beta-Bernoulli conjugate update
rng = np.random.default_rng(0)

# Observed data: variant A converted 45/500, variant B converted 60/520
alpha_prior, beta_prior = 1, 1     # uniform (uninformative) prior
a_successes, a_trials = 45, 500
b_successes, b_trials = 60, 520

post_a = stats.beta(alpha_prior + a_successes, beta_prior + a_trials - a_successes)
post_b = stats.beta(alpha_prior + b_successes, beta_prior + b_trials - b_successes)

# Monte Carlo estimate of P(B > A) by sampling both posteriors
samples_a = post_a.rvs(100_000, random_state=rng)
samples_b = post_b.rvs(100_000, random_state=rng)
p_b_better = (samples_b > samples_a).mean()

print(f"P(variant B has higher true conversion rate) ≈ {p_b_better:.3f}")
print(f"Posterior mean A: {post_a.mean():.4f}, B: {post_b.mean():.4f}")
```

## Complexity

| Approach | Cost | When to use |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Conjugate prior | Closed-form, $O(1)$ update per observation | Whenever likelihood/prior form a known conjugate pair |
| MAP estimation | Same cost as MLE (point optimization) | Fast point estimate with regularization interpretation |
| Grid approximation | $O(g^d)$ for $g$ grid points, $d$ dimensions | Low-dimensional posteriors only |
| MCMC (Metropolis-Hastings, HMC) | Many likelihood evaluations per sample | General posteriors, no conjugacy required |
| Variational inference | Optimization-based, faster than MCMC, approximate | Large-scale/deep Bayesian models |

## Common Mistakes

<ProsCons
  pros={[
    "Using conjugate priors when applicable — they turn Bayesian updating into simple parameter arithmetic instead of requiring MCMC.",
    "Reporting full posterior distributions (or credible intervals) rather than collapsing to MAP when uncertainty quantification matters downstream.",
  ]}
  cons={[
    "Confusing a Bayesian credible interval ('there's a 95% probability θ is in this range, given the data and prior') with a frequentist confidence interval ('95% of such intervals constructed this way would contain the true θ across repeated sampling') — they answer different questions and are numerically different in general.",
    "Choosing an 'uninformative' prior without checking it's actually uninformative in the parameterization that matters — e.g. a uniform prior on θ is not uniform on log-odds or on θ².",
    "Ignoring that MAP still discards uncertainty — for downstream decisions sensitive to tail risk, using only the MAP estimate can be as overconfident as plain MLE.",
  ]}
