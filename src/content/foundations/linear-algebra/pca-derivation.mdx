## Intuition

You have data in high dimensions and want to compress it into fewer dimensions while losing as little information as possible. PCA answers a very specific version of that question: find the direction along which the data varies the most, project onto it, then find the next-best direction orthogonal to the first, and so on. The remarkable fact — which we'll derive rigorously below — is that this "maximize variance" goal turns out to be solved *exactly* by the eigenvectors of the data's covariance matrix.

<Callout type="tip" title="One-sentence definition">
  The principal components of a dataset are the eigenvectors of its covariance matrix, ordered by eigenvalue (variance explained); projecting onto the top $k$ components is the best possible rank-$k$ linear compression of the data in a mean-squared-error sense.
</Callout>

This page assumes you're comfortable with [Eigenvalues & Eigenvectors](/docs/foundations/linear-algebra/eigenvalues-eigenvectors) and derives PCA from first principles using constrained optimization — not just stating the recipe, but proving why it's correct.

## Setup

Let $X \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times d}$ be a data matrix with $n$ samples and $d$ features, assumed **centered** (each column has mean zero — subtract the column means first if not). The (empirical) covariance matrix is:

$$
\Sigma = \frac{1}{n-1} X^\top X \in \mathbb{R}^{d \times d}
$$

$\Sigma$ is always symmetric positive semi-definite: symmetric because $\Sigma^\top = \Sigma$ trivially, and PSD because for any $v$, $v^\top \Sigma v = \frac{1}{n-1}\|Xv\|^2 \ge 0$. By the spectral theorem (see the eigenvalues page), $\Sigma$ therefore has real, nonnegative eigenvalues and an orthonormal set of eigenvectors.

## Derivation 1: Maximum Variance Formulation

**Goal:** find a unit vector $v$ ($\|v\|=1$) such that projecting the centered data onto $v$ maximizes the variance of the projected values.

The projection of a single centered sample $x_i$ onto direction $v$ is the scalar $x_i^\top v$. The variance of these projected values across all $n$ samples is:

$$
\text{Var}(Xv) = \frac{1}{n-1}\sum_{i=1}^n (x_i^\top v)^2 = \frac{1}{n-1} \|Xv\|^2 = \frac{1}{n-1} v^\top X^\top X v = v^\top \Sigma v
$$

(using that the data is centered, so the mean of $x_i^\top v$ is zero, and variance reduces to the mean of squares).

**Step 1 — set up the constrained optimization.**

$$
\max_v \; v^\top \Sigma v \quad \text{subject to} \quad v^\top v = 1
$$

The unit-norm constraint is essential — without it, $v^\top \Sigma v$ is unbounded (just scale $v$ up).

**Step 2 — form the Lagrangian.**

$$
\mathcal{L}(v, \lambda) = v^\top \Sigma v - \lambda(v^\top v - 1)
$$

**Step 3 — take the gradient and set it to zero.**

$$
\nabla_v \mathcal{L} = 2\Sigma v - 2\lambda v = 0 \implies \Sigma v = \lambda v
$$

This is exactly the eigenvector equation. So any **critical point** of the constrained problem is an eigenvector of $\Sigma$, with $\lambda$ its corresponding eigenvalue.

**Step 4 — identify which eigenvector maximizes the objective.** At a critical point $v$ with $\Sigma v = \lambda v$, the objective value itself is:

$$
v^\top \Sigma v = v^\top (\lambda v) = \lambda (v^\top v) = \lambda
$$

So the variance captured along direction $v$ is *exactly* its eigenvalue $\lambda$. To maximize variance, pick the eigenvector with the **largest** eigenvalue $\lambda_1$. This is the **first principal component**.

**Step 5 — the second component.** Repeat the maximization, now additionally constrained to be orthogonal to the first component ($v^\top v_1 = 0$). Adding this constraint to the Lagrangian and redoing the same stationarity argument shows the maximizer is the eigenvector with the second-largest eigenvalue, $v_2$. Inductively, the $k$-th principal component is the eigenvector of $\Sigma$ with the $k$-th largest eigenvalue, and the total variance captured by the top $k$ components is $\sum_{i=1}^k \lambda_i$.

## Derivation 2: Minimum Reconstruction Error (Equivalent View)

A seemingly different goal: choose an orthonormal basis $v_1, \ldots, v_k$ minimizing the squared reconstruction error when each point is projected onto $\text{span}(v_1,\ldots,v_k)$ and mapped back:

$$
\min_{\{v_i\}} \sum_{i=1}^n \left\| x_i - \sum_{j=1}^k (x_i^\top v_j) v_j \right\|^2
$$

Expanding the squared norm and using orthonormality of the $v_j$, one can show this error decomposes as:

$$
\sum_{i=1}^n \|x_i\|^2 - (n-1)\sum_{j=1}^k v_j^\top \Sigma v_j
$$

The first term is a fixed constant (total variance in the data, independent of the choice of $v_j$'s). So **minimizing reconstruction error is equivalent to maximizing** $\sum_j v_j^\top \Sigma v_j$ — exactly the same objective as Derivation 1. Both formulations are solved by the same eigenvectors of $\Sigma$. This equivalence is why PCA is simultaneously described as "maximize variance" and "minimize reconstruction error" in different textbooks — they are the same optimization problem in disguise.

## Connection to SVD

Directly eigendecomposing $\Sigma = \frac{1}{n-1}X^\top X$ requires forming $X^\top X$ explicitly, which can be numerically unstable (it squares the condition number of $X$). In practice, PCA is computed via the [SVD](/docs/foundations/linear-algebra/svd) of $X$ itself:

$$
X = U \Sigma_{\text{svd}} V^\top \implies X^\top X = V \Sigma_{\text{svd}}^\top \Sigma_{\text{svd}} V^\top
$$

So the right singular vectors $V$ of $X$ are exactly the eigenvectors (principal components) of $\Sigma$, and the eigenvalues are $\lambda_i = \sigma_i^2/(n-1)$ where $\sigma_i$ are $X$'s singular values. This is the numerically preferred route — compute the SVD of the (centered) data matrix directly rather than forming and eigendecomposing $X^\top X$.

## Worked Example

Data (already centered), 4 points in $\mathbb{R}^2$:

$$
X = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 1 \\ -1 & -2 \\ 1 & 0 \\ -2 & 1 \end{pmatrix}
$$

**Step 1 — covariance matrix.** $X^\top X = \begin{pmatrix} 2^2+(-1)^2+1^2+(-2)^2 & 2(1)+(-1)(-2)+1(0)+(-2)(1) \\ \cdot & 1^2+(-2)^2+0^2+1^2\end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix}10 & 2\\2 & 6\end{pmatrix}$.

With $n=4$: $\Sigma = \frac{1}{3}\begin{pmatrix}10 & 2\\2 & 6\end{pmatrix}$.

**Step 2 — eigenvalues of $\begin{pmatrix}10&2\\2&6\end{pmatrix}$** (we can eigendecompose before dividing by 3, then divide eigenvalues by 3 at the end): characteristic polynomial $\lambda^2 - 16\lambda + (60-4) = \lambda^2-16\lambda+56=0$. Roots: $\lambda = \frac{16 \pm \sqrt{256-224}}{2} = \frac{16\pm\sqrt{32}}{2} = 8 \pm 2\sqrt{2}$, i.e. $\lambda_1 \approx 10.83$, $\lambda_2 \approx 5.17$. Dividing by $n-1=3$: the covariance eigenvalues are $\approx 3.61$ and $\approx 1.72$.

**Step 3 — first principal component** solves $(10-\lambda_1)v_1 + 2v_2 = 0$ with $\lambda_1 \approx 10.83$: $v_2 \approx 0.414\, v_1$, giving (normalized) $v^{(1)} \approx (0.925, 0.383)^\top$ — the direction along which this data varies most, tilted slightly toward the first coordinate since it has larger individual variance.

## Code Example

```python
import numpy as np

X = np.array([
    [2.0,  1.0],
    [-1.0, -2.0],
    [1.0,  0.0],
    [-2.0, 1.0],
])

# Center (already centered here, but always do this in practice)
X_centered = X - X.mean(axis=0)

# Method 1: eigendecomposition of the covariance matrix
cov = np.cov(X_centered, rowvar=False)          # (2, 2)
eigvals, eigvecs = np.linalg.eigh(cov)          # eigh: for symmetric matrices, ascending order
order = np.argsort(eigvals)[::-1]               # sort descending
eigvals, eigvecs = eigvals[order], eigvecs[:, order]
print("eigenvalues:", eigvals)
print("principal components (columns):\n", eigvecs)

# Method 2: SVD of the data matrix directly (numerically preferred)
U, s, Vt = np.linalg.svd(X_centered, full_matrices=False)
pca_eigenvalues_from_svd = (s ** 2) / (X.shape[0] - 1)
print("eigenvalues via SVD:", pca_eigenvalues_from_svd)
print("principal components via SVD (rows of Vt):\n", Vt)

# Project onto top-1 component
k = 1
X_reduced = X_centered @ eigvecs[:, :k]
print("reduced data:\n", X_reduced)
```

## Complexity

| Operation | Time complexity | Space |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Forming $\Sigma = X^\top X / (n-1)$ | $O(nd^2)$ | $O(d^2)$ |
| Eigendecomposition of $\Sigma$ (dense, $d \times d$) | $O(d^3)$ | $O(d^2)$ |
| SVD of $X$ directly (n×d, preferred) | $O(nd\min(n,d))$ | $O(nd)$ |
| Projecting $n$ points onto top-$k$ components | $O(ndk)$ | $O(nk)$ |
| Randomized PCA (top-$k$ only, large $d$) | $O(nd\log k + (n+d)k^2)$ approx | $O((n+d)k)$ |

## Common Mistakes

<ProsCons
  pros={[
    "Always centering (and often standardizing/scaling) the data before PCA — uncentered data corrupts the covariance-as-variance interpretation entirely.",
    "Using SVD of X directly instead of eigendecomposing X^T X for better numerical stability, especially when d is large or X is ill-conditioned.",
  ]}
  cons={[
    "Forgetting to standardize when features are on very different scales (e.g., income in dollars vs. age in years) — PCA will be dominated by whichever feature happens to have larger raw variance, not larger *informativeness*.",
    "Treating principal components as inherently interpretable — they're linear combinations of the original features and often don't correspond to any meaningful real-world concept.",
    "Assuming PCA finds the best features for a downstream supervised task — PCA only optimizes for variance/reconstruction, which is unsupervised and can discard directions that are low-variance but highly predictive of the label.",
  ]}
/>
