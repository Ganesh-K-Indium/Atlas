## Intuition

Eigendecomposition is powerful, but it has a restrictive requirement: the matrix must be square, and even then it isn't guaranteed to have a full set of real eigenvectors. The **Singular Value Decomposition** fixes both problems. It says something remarkable: *any* matrix at all — square, rectangular, singular, whatever — can be broken down into a rotation, a scaling along orthogonal axes, and another rotation.

<Callout type="tip" title="One-sentence definition">
  Every matrix $A$ can be written as $A = U\Sigma V^\top$: rotate the input space ($V^\top$), stretch along orthogonal axes by nonnegative amounts ($\Sigma$), then rotate into the output space ($U$).
</Callout>

Think of $A$ as a machine that takes a unit sphere in the input space and maps it to an ellipse (or ellipsoid) in the output space. SVD identifies exactly that ellipsoid: $V$'s columns are the input directions that map to the ellipsoid's axes, $U$'s columns are those axes' directions in the output space, and $\Sigma$'s diagonal entries are the axes' lengths. This geometric fact — "every linear map takes spheres to ellipsoids" — is the entire content of the theorem.

## Formal Definition

For any real matrix $A \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times n}$, there exist orthogonal matrices $U \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times m}$, $V \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times n}$ and a diagonal (rectangular) matrix $\Sigma \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times n}$ such that:

$$
A = U \Sigma V^\top
$$

- $U = [u_1, \ldots, u_m]$ has orthonormal columns ($U^\top U = I_m$) called **left singular vectors**.
- $V = [v_1, \ldots, v_n]$ has orthonormal columns ($V^\top V = I_n$) called **right singular vectors**.
- $\Sigma$ has nonnegative entries $\sigma_1 \ge \sigma_2 \ge \cdots \ge \sigma_r > 0$ on its diagonal (where $r = \text{rank}(A)$) and zeros elsewhere — the **singular values**.

Equivalently, in outer-product form:

$$
A = \sum_{i=1}^r \sigma_i\, u_i v_i^\top
$$

— $A$ is a sum of $r$ rank-1 matrices, each weighted by a singular value, ordered by decreasing importance. This form is exactly what makes low-rank approximation possible: truncate the sum after $k < r$ terms and you get the best possible rank-$k$ approximation to $A$ (in the least-squares sense — the **Eckart–Young theorem**).

### Connection to Eigendecomposition

SVD and eigendecomposition are related but distinct: SVD exists for *any* matrix (even non-square, even singular), while eigendecomposition requires a square matrix and isn't always available in real form.

The connection: consider $A^\top A \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times n}$, which is always symmetric positive semi-definite. Substituting $A = U\Sigma V^\top$:

$$
A^\top A = (U\Sigma V^\top)^\top (U\Sigma V^\top) = V \Sigma^\top U^\top U \Sigma V^\top = V \Sigma^\top \Sigma V^\top = V (\Sigma^\top \Sigma) V^\top
$$

using $U^\top U = I$. This is exactly the eigendecomposition of $A^\top A$: the columns of $V$ are its eigenvectors, and $\Sigma^\top\Sigma$'s diagonal entries ($\sigma_i^2$) are its eigenvalues. Symmetrically, the columns of $U$ are the eigenvectors of $AA^\top$. So:

$$
\sigma_i = \sqrt{\lambda_i(A^\top A)}, \qquad v_i = \text{eigenvector of } A^\top A, \qquad u_i = \text{eigenvector of } AA^\top
$$

For a symmetric positive semi-definite matrix specifically, the SVD and the eigendecomposition coincide exactly ($U = V$, $\Sigma = \Lambda$) — see [Eigenvalues & Eigenvectors](/docs/foundations/linear-algebra/eigenvalues-eigenvectors) for why symmetric matrices guarantee real eigenvalues and orthogonal eigenvectors in the first place.

## Derivation: SVD of a 2×3 Matrix

Let $A = \begin{pmatrix} 3 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 2 \end{pmatrix}$.

**Step 1 — compute $A A^\top$ (2×2, gives $U$ and $\Sigma^2$):**

$$
AA^\top = \begin{pmatrix} 3 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 2 \end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix} 3 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 2 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 9 & 0 \\ 0 & 4 \end{pmatrix}
$$

This is already diagonal, so its eigenvalues are $9$ and $4$ with eigenvectors $e_1 = (1,0)^\top$ and $e_2 = (0,1)^\top$. So $U = I_2$ and singular values $\sigma_1 = 3, \sigma_2 = 2$ (already sorted descending).

**Step 2 — compute $A^\top A$ (3×3, gives $V$):**

$$
A^\top A = \begin{pmatrix} 3 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 2 \end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix} 3 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 2 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 9 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 4 \end{pmatrix}
$$

Eigenvalues $9, 4, 0$ with eigenvectors $(1,0,0)^\top$, $(0,0,1)^\top$, $(0,1,0)^\top$ respectively. Ordering by descending eigenvalue: $v_1 = (1,0,0)^\top$, $v_2 = (0,0,1)^\top$, $v_3 = (0,1,0)^\top$ (the third, for $\sigma=0$, spans the null space of $A$).

**Step 3 — assemble.**

$$
U = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix}, \quad
\Sigma = \begin{pmatrix} 3 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 2 & 0 \end{pmatrix}, \quad
V^\top = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \\ 0 & 1 & 0 \end{pmatrix}
$$

**Check:** $U\Sigma V^\top = \begin{pmatrix}1&0\\0&1\end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix}3&0&0\\0&2&0\end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix}1&0&0\\0&0&1\\0&1&0\end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix}3&0&0\\0&2&0\end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix}1&0&0\\0&0&1\\0&1&0\end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix}3&0&0\\0&0&2\end{pmatrix} = A$ ✓

## Low-Rank Approximation (Eckart–Young)

Truncating the sum $A = \sum_i \sigma_i u_i v_i^\top$ to the top $k$ terms gives:

$$
A_k = \sum_{i=1}^k \sigma_i u_i v_i^\top
$$

The Eckart–Young theorem states $A_k$ is the best rank-$k$ approximation to $A$ under both the Frobenius norm and the spectral (operator) norm:

$$
A_k = \underset{\text{rank}(B) \le k}{\arg\min} \|A - B\|_F
$$

with approximation error $\|A - A_k\|_F = \sqrt{\sum_{i=k+1}^r \sigma_i^2}$. This is precisely why truncated SVD is used for compression: keep only the largest singular values/vectors and discard the rest, with a *provably* minimal loss for that rank budget.

## Code Example

```python
import numpy as np

A = np.array([[3.0, 0.0, 0.0],
              [0.0, 0.0, 2.0]])

U, s, Vt = np.linalg.svd(A, full_matrices=True)
print("U:\n", U)
print("singular values:", s)      # [3. 2.]
print("Vt:\n", Vt)

# Reconstruct A from the factors
Sigma = np.zeros_like(A)
np.fill_diagonal(Sigma, s)
A_reconstructed = U @ Sigma @ Vt
print(np.allclose(A, A_reconstructed))  # True

# Rank-k truncated SVD for low-rank approximation / compression
def truncated_svd(A, k):
    U, s, Vt = np.linalg.svd(A, full_matrices=False)
    return U[:, :k] @ np.diag(s[:k]) @ Vt[:k, :]

A_rank1 = truncated_svd(A, k=1)
print(A_rank1)  # best rank-1 approximation of A
```

## Complexity

| Operation | Time complexity | Space |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Full SVD, dense $m \times n$ (via bidiagonalization + QR-like iteration) | $O(mn \cdot \min(m,n))$ | $O(mn)$ |
| Truncated SVD (top-$k$, via randomized algorithms) | $O(mn\,k)$ approximately | $O((m+n)k)$ |
| Reconstructing $A$ from top-$k$ factors | $O(mnk)$ | $O((m+n)k)$ |

## Common Mistakes

<ProsCons
  pros={[
    "Using SVD (not eigendecomposition) whenever the matrix is non-square or you need guaranteed real, nonnegative scaling factors.",
    "Reaching for truncated/randomized SVD for large matrices instead of computing the full decomposition when only the top-k components are needed.",
  ]}
  cons={[
    "Confusing singular values with eigenvalues in general — they coincide only for symmetric PSD matrices; for a general matrix, eigenvalues can even be negative or complex while singular values are always real and nonnegative.",
    "Assuming U and V are the same matrix — they're generally different, living in different spaces (R^m and R^n respectively) unless A is square and symmetric PSD.",
    "Forgetting that the 'thin'/'economy' SVD (only the first r or k columns of U and V) is what libraries return by default in some conventions — always check whether full_matrices is True or False.",
  ]}
