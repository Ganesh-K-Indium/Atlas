## Intuition

Unconstrained optimization asks "where is the slope zero?" Constrained optimization asks something subtler: "where is the slope zero *among the directions I'm still allowed to move in*?" At a constrained optimum, you might be sitting at a point where the objective's gradient is nonzero, but every direction that would decrease it is blocked by a constraint — like a ball resting against a wall on a slope. Lagrange multipliers formalize "blocked by a constraint" as a precise algebraic condition: the objective's gradient must be expressible as a combination of the constraints' gradients.

<Callout type="tip" title="The one idea that matters">
  At a constrained optimum, the objective's gradient is forced to be a linear combination of the active constraints' gradients — because if it weren't, there would be a feasible direction that still decreases the objective, contradicting optimality. The KKT conditions are simply this idea made precise and extended to inequality constraints.
</Callout>

## Formal Setup: Equality Constraints

Consider:

$$
\min_x f(x) \quad \text{subject to} \quad h(x) = 0
$$

for $f: \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}$ and $h: \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}$ (a single equality constraint, for clarity). Define the **Lagrangian**:

$$
\mathcal{L}(x, \lambda) = f(x) + \lambda \, h(x)
$$

**Why stationarity of $\mathcal{L}$ characterizes constrained optima.** Suppose $x^*$ is a local minimum of $f$ subject to $h(x)=0$, and suppose $\nabla h(x^*) \ne 0$ (a regularity condition). Consider moving along the constraint surface: any feasible direction $v$ at $x^*$ must satisfy $\nabla h(x^*)^\top v = 0$ (to first order, staying on the surface $h=0$). If $\nabla f(x^*)$ had a nonzero component orthogonal to $\nabla h(x^*)$, that component would itself be a feasible direction (tangent to the constraint surface) along which $f$ strictly decreases — contradicting that $x^*$ is a constrained minimum. So $\nabla f(x^*)$ must be **parallel** to $\nabla h(x^*)$:

$$
\nabla f(x^*) = -\lambda^* \nabla h(x^*) \quad \Longleftrightarrow \quad \nabla_x \mathcal{L}(x^*, \lambda^*) = 0
$$

for some scalar $\lambda^*$ (the Lagrange multiplier). This, plus the constraint itself ($\nabla_\lambda \mathcal{L} = h(x^*) = 0$), gives the full first-order system.

### Worked example: max entropy subject to a normalization constraint

Maximize $-\sum_i p_i \log p_i$ subject to $\sum_i p_i = 1$. Lagrangian: $\mathcal{L}(p, \lambda) = -\sum_i p_i \log p_i + \lambda\left(\sum_i p_i - 1\right)$.

$$
\frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial p_i} = -\log p_i - 1 + \lambda = 0 \implies p_i = e^{\lambda - 1}
$$

Every $p_i$ takes the *same* value (independent of $i$) — so the constraint $\sum_i p_i = 1$ forces $p_i = 1/n$ for all $i$: the maximum-entropy distribution under only a normalization constraint is uniform, exactly matching intuition.

## Inequality Constraints and the KKT Conditions

Now consider the general nonlinear program:

$$
\min_x f(x) \quad \text{s.t.} \quad g_i(x) \le 0 \ (i=1,\dots,m), \qquad h_j(x) = 0 \ (j=1,\dots,p)
$$

Lagrangian: $\mathcal{L}(x, \mu, \lambda) = f(x) + \sum_i \mu_i g_i(x) + \sum_j \lambda_j h_j(x)$.

The **Karush-Kuhn-Tucker (KKT) conditions** are necessary for optimality at $x^*$ (under a constraint qualification, e.g. Slater's condition for convex problems):

1. **Stationarity:** $\nabla_x \mathcal{L}(x^*, \mu^*, \lambda^*) = \nabla f(x^*) + \sum_i \mu_i^* \nabla g_i(x^*) + \sum_j \lambda_j^* \nabla h_j(x^*) = 0$
2. **Primal feasibility:** $g_i(x^*) \le 0$, $h_j(x^*) = 0$ for all $i,j$
3. **Dual feasibility:** $\mu_i^* \ge 0$ for all $i$
4. **Complementary slackness:** $\mu_i^* \, g_i(x^*) = 0$ for all $i$

**Why $\mu_i \ge 0$ (dual feasibility) is required, but no such sign constraint exists for $\lambda_j$.** An equality constraint $h_j(x)=0$ can be pushed from either side, so its multiplier can have either sign. But an inequality constraint $g_i(x) \le 0$ only blocks movement in the direction that would *increase* $g_i$ past zero; pushing the objective's gradient in the "wrong" sign along $\nabla g_i$ would make the constraint non-binding or infeasible in a way that couldn't correspond to an optimum, so the KKT derivation forces $\mu_i \ge 0$.

**Why complementary slackness makes sense.** If a constraint is *inactive* ($g_i(x^*) < 0$, strictly interior), it isn't blocking any direction, so its multiplier should be $0$ — irrelevant to the local geometry. If it's *active* ($g_i(x^*) = 0$), it may have $\mu_i > 0$, meaning it genuinely constrains the optimum (it's "pushing back"). Complementary slackness ($\mu_i g_i(x^*) = 0$) encodes exactly this: at least one of "constraint slack" or "multiplier" must be zero at every $i$.

## Worked Example: Support Vector Machine

The hard-margin SVM primal problem is $\min_{w,b} \frac{1}{2}\|w\|^2$ subject to $y_i(w^\top x_i + b) \ge 1$ for all $i$, i.e., $g_i(w,b) = 1 - y_i(w^\top x_i + b) \le 0$.

Lagrangian: $\mathcal{L}(w,b,\mu) = \frac{1}{2}\|w\|^2 + \sum_i \mu_i \big(1 - y_i(w^\top x_i + b)\big)$, $\mu_i \ge 0$.

Stationarity conditions:

$$
\nabla_w \mathcal{L} = w - \sum_i \mu_i y_i x_i = 0 \implies w^* = \sum_i \mu_i y_i x_i
$$
$$
\frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial b} = -\sum_i \mu_i y_i = 0 \implies \sum_i \mu_i y_i = 0
$$

**Complementary slackness** ($\mu_i (1 - y_i(w^\top x_i + b)) = 0$) says: for any point strictly outside the margin ($y_i(w^\top x_i+b) > 1$), $\mu_i = 0$ — it has *no influence* on $w^*$. Only points exactly on the margin ($\mu_i$ possibly $> 0$) contribute to $w^* = \sum_i \mu_i y_i x_i$. This is precisely why they're called **support vectors**: the KKT conditions prove, from first principles, that the decision boundary depends only on the points touching the margin, not the full dataset.

## Code Example

```python
import numpy as np
from scipy.optimize import minimize

# Example: minimize f(x,y) = x^2 + y^2 subject to x + y = 1 (equality constraint)
# Analytic answer via Lagrange: grad f = lambda * grad h => (2x, 2y) = lambda*(1,1) => x = y
# Combined with x + y = 1 => x = y = 0.5

def f(v):
    x, y = v
    return x**2 + y**2

constraints = [{"type": "eq", "fun": lambda v: v[0] + v[1] - 1}]
result = minimize(f, x0=np.array([0.0, 0.0]), constraints=constraints)
print(result.x)  # -> [0.5, 0.5], matching the closed-form Lagrange solution

# Inequality-constrained example: minimize x^2 + y^2 subject to x + y >= 1
# (g(x,y) = 1 - x - y <= 0). KKT predicts the same solution as equality here,
# since the unconstrained minimum (0,0) violates x+y>=1, so the constraint is active.
constraints_ineq = [{"type": "ineq", "fun": lambda v: v[0] + v[1] - 1}]
result_ineq = minimize(f, x0=np.array([0.0, 0.0]), constraints=constraints_ineq)
print(result_ineq.x)  # -> [0.5, 0.5] as well; constraint is active (binding)
```

## Complexity

| Problem structure | Typical solution method | Complexity notes |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Convex, equality constraints only | Solve linear KKT system directly | $O(n^3)$ for a dense $n \times n$ linear system (e.g. via elimination) |
| Convex, with inequality constraints | Interior-point methods (log-barrier) | $O(\sqrt{m})$ Newton iterations for $m$ constraints, each $O(n^3)$ |
| Non-convex, general nonlinear program | Sequential Quadratic Programming (SQP), penalty/augmented Lagrangian methods | No global guarantee; converges to a KKT point (locally optimal candidate) |

## Common Mistakes

<ProsCons
  pros={[
    "KKT conditions turn a constrained problem into an algebraic system, enabling closed-form or numerically efficient solutions (e.g. interior-point methods).",
    "For convex problems, KKT gives a certificate of global optimality — not just a local one.",
  ]}
  cons={[
    "Forgetting the sign constraint μᵢ ≥ 0 for inequality multipliers (unlike equality multipliers, which are unrestricted in sign) — flipping this sign silently produces a maximization solution or an invalid candidate.",
    "Applying KKT necessary conditions to a non-convex problem and assuming the resulting stationary point is a global (or even local) minimum — without convexity, KKT points can be saddle points or local maxima too.",
    "Ignoring constraint qualification (e.g. Slater's condition, or linear independence of active constraint gradients) — without it, KKT conditions may fail to hold at the true optimum, or a KKT point may not actually be optimal.",
    "Assuming complementary slackness means 'inactive constraints don't matter at all' — inactive constraints can still become active as the problem or data changes, which matters for sensitivity analysis (the multiplier value itself is the shadow price / sensitivity of the optimum to relaxing the constraint).",
  ]}
/>
