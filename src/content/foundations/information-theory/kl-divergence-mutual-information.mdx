## Intuition

If entropy measures the surprise inherent in one distribution, **KL divergence** measures the *extra* surprise you incur from using the wrong distribution to make predictions — it's a way of quantifying "how different is my belief $q$ from reality $p$," expressed in the same information units (bits/nats) as entropy.

It behaves almost like a distance — it's zero exactly when the two distributions are identical, and positive otherwise — but it is **not symmetric** ($\mathrm{KL}(p\|q) \neq \mathrm{KL}(q\|p)$ in general) and doesn't satisfy the triangle inequality, so mathematically it's called a *divergence*, not a metric.

**Mutual information** asks a related but distinct question: for two random variables $X$ and $Y$, how much does observing one tell you about the other? It turns out this is exactly the KL divergence between their true joint distribution and the (hypothetical) distribution where they'd be independent — i.e., mutual information measures divergence from independence.

<Callout type="tip" title="The one-sentence idea">
  KL divergence is the expected extra number of bits you waste by encoding/predicting with q when the truth is p, and mutual information is just KL divergence applied to measure how far a joint distribution is from treating its two variables as independent.
</Callout>

## Formal Definition

For discrete distributions $p$ and $q$ over the same support, the **Kullback-Leibler divergence** is:

$$
\mathrm{KL}(p \,\|\, q) = \sum_x p(x) \log \frac{p(x)}{q(x)} = \mathbb{E}_{x\sim p}\left[\log \frac{p(x)}{q(x)}\right]
$$

with the conventions $0 \log \frac{0}{q} = 0$ and $\mathrm{KL}(p\|q) = +\infty$ if there is any $x$ with $p(x) > 0$ but $q(x) = 0$ (a genuinely infinite penalty for ruling out something that actually happens).

For two random variables $X, Y$ with joint distribution $p(x,y)$ and marginals $p(x), p(y)$, the **mutual information** is:

$$
I(X;Y) = \sum_{x,y} p(x,y)\log\frac{p(x,y)}{p(x)p(y)} = \mathrm{KL}\big(p(x,y) \,\big\|\, p(x)p(y)\big)
$$

## Derivation

### Non-negativity of KL divergence, via Jensen's inequality

Jensen's inequality states that for a convex function $f$ and random variable $Z$, $\mathbb{E}[f(Z)] \geq f(\mathbb{E}[Z])$. The function $-\log$ is convex, so we apply Jensen with $Z = q(x)/p(x)$ (a ratio, evaluated under expectation over $x \sim p$):

$$
\begin{aligned}
\mathrm{KL}(p\|q) &= \sum_x p(x)\log\frac{p(x)}{q(x)} = \sum_x p(x)\left(-\log\frac{q(x)}{p(x)}\right) = \mathbb{E}_{x\sim p}\left[-\log\frac{q(x)}{p(x)}\right] \\
&\geq -\log\left(\mathbb{E}_{x\sim p}\left[\frac{q(x)}{p(x)}\right]\right) \qquad \text{(Jensen, since } -\log \text{ is convex)}\\
&= -\log\left(\sum_x p(x)\cdot\frac{q(x)}{p(x)}\right) = -\log\left(\sum_x q(x)\right) = -\log(1) = 0
\end{aligned}
$$

The sum $\sum_x q(x) = 1$ collapses because $q$ is a valid probability distribution. This proves $\mathrm{KL}(p\|q) \geq 0$ for any two distributions $p, q$ over the same support.

**When is equality achieved?** Jensen's inequality is tight exactly when the random variable inside the expectation is almost-surely constant — here, when $q(x)/p(x)$ is the same constant for every $x$ with $p(x) > 0$. Since both distributions sum to 1, that constant must be exactly 1, i.e. $q(x) = p(x)$ everywhere. So $\mathrm{KL}(p\|q) = 0 \iff p = q$ — strict positivity otherwise, which is exactly what makes KL divergence useful as a (asymmetric) dissimilarity measure.

### Why KL divergence is asymmetric

$\mathrm{KL}(p\|q)$ weights the log-ratio by $p(x)$; $\mathrm{KL}(q\|p)$ weights the same log-ratio (with sign flipped) by $q(x)$ instead. These are different weighted averages of different quantities in general, so there's no reason for them to coincide. Concretely, a useful intuition:

- $\mathrm{KL}(p\|q)$ (the "forward KL," used e.g. in maximum likelihood fitting) heavily penalizes $q(x)$ being near zero wherever $p(x)$ is non-negligible — it forces $q$ to cover all the support of $p$ ("mode-covering," can look overly spread out if $p$ is multimodal).
- $\mathrm{KL}(q\|p)$ (the "reverse KL," common in variational inference) is only large where $q$ puts mass but $p$ doesn't — it lets $q$ ignore regions of $p$'s support ("mode-seeking," tends to lock onto a single mode of $p$).

Because of this asymmetry, KL divergence is not a metric: it also fails the triangle inequality in general, which is why it's called a *divergence*.

### Mutual information as KL divergence from independence

If $X$ and $Y$ were truly independent, their joint distribution would factor exactly as $p(x)p(y)$. Mutual information measures how far the *actual* joint $p(x,y)$ is from this hypothetical independent joint, using KL divergence as the distance:

$$
I(X;Y) = \mathrm{KL}\big(p(x,y)\,\|\,p(x)p(y)\big) = \sum_{x,y}p(x,y)\log\frac{p(x,y)}{p(x)p(y)} \geq 0
$$

Non-negativity follows immediately from the KL non-negativity result above (with $p \to p(x,y)$ and $q \to p(x)p(y)$), and $I(X;Y) = 0 \iff p(x,y) = p(x)p(y)$ everywhere, i.e. **iff $X$ and $Y$ are independent** — exactly matching the intuition that independent variables carry zero information about each other.

This identity also connects mutual information back to entropy. Expanding the log-ratio:

$$
\begin{aligned}
I(X;Y) &= \sum_{x,y} p(x,y)\log\frac{p(x,y)}{p(x)p(y)} = \sum_{x,y}p(x,y)\log\frac{p(x\mid y)}{p(x)}\\
&= \sum_{x,y}p(x,y)\log p(x\mid y) \;-\;\sum_{x,y}p(x,y)\log p(x) \\
&= -H(X\mid Y) - \big(-H(X)\big) = H(X) - H(X\mid Y)
\end{aligned}
$$

So mutual information is exactly **the reduction in uncertainty about $X$ once you observe $Y$**: how many bits of "surprise about X" you eliminate, on average, by learning $Y$. By symmetry of the joint-vs-marginals definition, $I(X;Y) = I(Y;X)$ — mutual information, unlike KL divergence in general, *is* symmetric, since swapping the roles of $X$ and $Y$ leaves $p(x,y)$ and $p(x)p(y)$ unchanged as sets.

## Code Example

```python
import numpy as np

def kl_divergence(p, q, base=2, eps=1e-12):
    """KL(p || q) for discrete distributions given as arrays over the same support."""
    p = np.asarray(p, dtype=float)
    q = np.clip(np.asarray(q, dtype=float), eps, 1.0)
    mask = p > 0                      # 0 * log(0/q) := 0
    return np.sum(p[mask] * np.log(p[mask] / q[mask]) / np.log(base))

def mutual_information(joint, base=2):
    """I(X;Y) from a joint probability table joint[i, j] = P(X=i, Y=j)."""
    joint = np.asarray(joint, dtype=float)
    px = joint.sum(axis=1, keepdims=True)   # marginal P(X)
    py = joint.sum(axis=0, keepdims=True)   # marginal P(Y)
    outer = px @ py                          # product of marginals, P(X)P(Y)
    return kl_divergence(joint.ravel(), outer.ravel(), base=base)

# Demonstrate asymmetry of KL
p = np.array([0.10, 0.40, 0.50])
q = np.array([0.60, 0.25, 0.15])
print("KL(p||q) =", kl_divergence(p, q))
print("KL(q||p) =", kl_divergence(q, p))   # different from KL(p||q)
print("KL(p||p) =", kl_divergence(p, p))   # exactly 0

# Mutual information: correlated joint vs. independent joint
joint_correlated = np.array([[0.4, 0.1],
                              [0.1, 0.4]])   # X and Y strongly agree
joint_independent = np.outer([0.5, 0.5], [0.5, 0.5])  # truly independent

print("I(X;Y) correlated  =", mutual_information(joint_correlated), "bits")
print("I(X;Y) independent =", mutual_information(joint_independent), "bits")  # ~0
```

## Common Mistakes

- **Calling KL divergence a "distance."** It's non-negative and zero only at equality (like a distance), but it's asymmetric and violates the triangle inequality, so it is formally a *divergence*, a weaker notion.
- **Mixing up forward and reverse KL in variational inference.** Choosing $\mathrm{KL}(q\|p)$ vs. $\mathrm{KL}(p\|q)$ as the objective produces qualitatively different approximate posteriors (mode-seeking vs. mode-covering) — this isn't a minor implementation detail, it changes what the fitted approximation looks like.
- **Forgetting the support condition.** If $q(x) = 0$ somewhere $p(x) > 0$, $\mathrm{KL}(p\|q) = \infty$. This shows up in practice as numerical blowups or `NaN` losses when a model assigns literal zero probability to something that occurs in the data — always add a small epsilon or work in log-space.
- **Assuming zero mutual information means "no relationship."** $I(X;Y) = 0$ only tests statistical independence, but it correctly captures *any* form of dependence (linear or not) — unlike Pearson correlation, which can be zero even for strongly, non-linearly dependent variables. It's a common misconception in the other direction: don't assume MI is "better than correlation" for small-sample estimation — MI is notoriously hard to estimate accurately from limited samples, especially in continuous or high-cardinality settings.
- **Computing KL/MI without handling zero-probability bins.** Naively computing `log(p/q)` when either is exactly 0 produces `-inf`/`NaN`; always mask or clip as shown in the code example.
