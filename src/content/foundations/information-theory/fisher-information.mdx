## Intuition

Imagine two possible likelihood functions for a parameter $\theta$: one is a sharp, narrow peak around the true value; the other is a broad, flat plateau. Data generated under the sharp-peaked likelihood pins down $\theta$ precisely — small changes in $\theta$ produce large changes in how likely the observed data looks, so the data is very informative about $\theta$. Data generated under the flat likelihood barely constrains $\theta$ at all — many different values of $\theta$ explain the data almost equally well.

**Fisher information** formalizes "how sharply peaked the likelihood is" as a single number (or matrix, for multiple parameters): it measures the expected curvature of the log-likelihood around the true parameter. High curvature (sharp peak) means high Fisher information means an estimator *can* be very precise. This single quantity turns out to do double duty: it sets the theoretical best-possible precision for *any* unbiased estimator (the Cramér-Rao bound), and it defines the natural geometry of parameter space that natural gradient descent exploits to take better-conditioned optimization steps than plain gradient descent.

<Callout type="tip" title="The one-sentence idea">
  Fisher information is the expected curvature (negative expected Hessian) of the log-likelihood with respect to the parameters — high curvature means the data pins the parameter down tightly, which both bounds how precise any estimator can be and tells second-order optimizers how to rescale their steps.
</Callout>

## Formal Definition

For a parametric family of distributions $p(x \mid \theta)$ with a scalar parameter $\theta$, the **score function** is the gradient of the log-likelihood:

$$
s(\theta; x) = \frac{\partial}{\partial \theta} \log p(x\mid\theta)
$$

The **Fisher information** is the variance of the score, equivalently its second moment (since $\mathbb{E}[s(\theta;x)] = 0$, shown below):

$$
\mathcal{I}(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_{x\sim p(\cdot\mid\theta)}\left[\left(\frac{\partial}{\partial\theta}\log p(x\mid\theta)\right)^2\right] = \mathrm{Var}\big[s(\theta;x)\big]
$$

For a vector parameter $\theta \in \mathbb{R}^d$, this generalizes to the **Fisher information matrix**:

$$
\mathcal{I}(\theta)_{ij} = \mathbb{E}\left[\frac{\partial \log p(x\mid\theta)}{\partial\theta_i}\cdot\frac{\partial \log p(x\mid\theta)}{\partial\theta_j}\right]
$$

## Derivation

### Step 1: The score has zero mean

This is the key regularity fact that makes everything else work. Since $p(x\mid\theta)$ integrates to 1 for every $\theta$:

$$
\int p(x\mid\theta)\,dx = 1 \quad\implies\quad \frac{\partial}{\partial\theta}\int p(x\mid\theta)\,dx = 0
$$

Assuming we can swap the order of differentiation and integration (a standard regularity condition):

$$
\int \frac{\partial p(x\mid\theta)}{\partial\theta}\,dx = 0
$$

Using the identity $\frac{\partial p}{\partial\theta} = p \cdot \frac{\partial \log p}{\partial\theta}$ (the "log-derivative trick"):

$$
\int p(x\mid\theta)\frac{\partial\log p(x\mid\theta)}{\partial\theta}\,dx = \mathbb{E}\left[\frac{\partial\log p(x\mid\theta)}{\partial\theta}\right] = \mathbb{E}[s(\theta;x)] = 0
$$

So the score has mean zero, which is why $\mathcal{I}(\theta) = \mathbb{E}[s^2] = \mathrm{Var}[s]$ — the second moment equals the variance directly.

### Step 2: Fisher information equals negative expected curvature of the log-likelihood

Differentiate the zero-mean identity $\int p \cdot \frac{\partial \log p}{\partial\theta}\,dx = 0$ once more with respect to $\theta$, using the product rule:

$$
\frac{\partial}{\partial\theta}\int p\cdot\frac{\partial\log p}{\partial\theta}\,dx = \int\left[\frac{\partial p}{\partial\theta}\cdot\frac{\partial\log p}{\partial\theta} + p\cdot\frac{\partial^2\log p}{\partial\theta^2}\right]dx = 0
$$

Substitute $\frac{\partial p}{\partial\theta} = p\cdot\frac{\partial\log p}{\partial\theta}$ again in the first term:

$$
\int p\left(\frac{\partial\log p}{\partial\theta}\right)^2 dx \;+\; \int p\cdot\frac{\partial^2\log p}{\partial\theta^2}\,dx \;=\; 0
$$

The first integral is exactly $\mathcal{I}(\theta) = \mathbb{E}[s^2]$. Rearranging:

$$
\mathcal{I}(\theta) = -\mathbb{E}\left[\frac{\partial^2}{\partial\theta^2}\log p(x\mid\theta)\right] = -\mathbb{E}[H_{\log p}(\theta)]
$$

This gives the second, equally standard definition of Fisher information: **the negative expected Hessian of the log-likelihood.** This is the connection that makes Fisher information a *curvature* — a sharply curved (concave, large negative second derivative) log-likelihood around $\theta$ means a large $\mathcal{I}(\theta)$, i.e. a log-likelihood that drops off fast as you move away from the true parameter, meaning the data strongly constrains $\theta$.

### Step 3: The Cramér-Rao lower bound

Let $\hat\theta(x)$ be any unbiased estimator of $\theta$, meaning $\mathbb{E}[\hat\theta(x)] = \theta$ for all $\theta$. Differentiate this identity with respect to $\theta$:

$$
\frac{\partial}{\partial\theta}\int \hat\theta(x)\,p(x\mid\theta)\,dx = 1 \quad\implies\quad \int \hat\theta(x)\,p(x\mid\theta)\,s(\theta;x)\,dx = 1
$$

(using the log-derivative trick again). Since $\mathbb{E}[s(\theta;x)] = 0$ (Step 1), we can subtract $\theta \cdot \mathbb{E}[s] = 0$ from the left side without changing it:

$$
\mathbb{E}\big[(\hat\theta(x) - \theta)\,s(\theta;x)\big] = 1
$$

This is $\mathrm{Cov}(\hat\theta, s) = 1$. Now apply the Cauchy-Schwarz inequality, $\mathrm{Cov}(A,B)^2 \leq \mathrm{Var}(A)\mathrm{Var}(B)$:

$$
1 = \mathrm{Cov}(\hat\theta, s)^2 \leq \mathrm{Var}(\hat\theta)\cdot\mathrm{Var}(s) = \mathrm{Var}(\hat\theta)\cdot\mathcal{I}(\theta)
$$

Rearranging gives the **Cramér-Rao lower bound**:

$$
\mathrm{Var}(\hat\theta) \geq \frac{1}{\mathcal{I}(\theta)}
$$

No unbiased estimator can have variance below the reciprocal of the Fisher information — Fisher information isn't just an abstract curvature measure, it's a literal, achievable-in-the-limit floor on estimation precision. (Maximum likelihood estimators are asymptotically efficient — as sample size grows, their variance approaches this bound — which is one of the deepest reasons MLE is the default estimation procedure across statistics; see [Maximum Likelihood Estimation](/docs/foundations/probability-statistics/maximum-likelihood-estimation) (roadmap).)

### Step 4: Fisher information as a Riemannian metric — the link to natural gradient descent

Ordinary gradient descent on a log-likelihood (or any loss) treats parameter space as flat Euclidean space: a step of fixed Euclidean size $\|\Delta\theta\|$ is treated as "the same size" everywhere, regardless of how much that step actually changes the model's output distribution. But two parameter settings can be numerically close while producing very different distributions (if Fisher information is high there), or numerically far apart while producing nearly identical distributions (if Fisher information is low there).

Locally, the KL divergence between $p(\cdot\mid\theta)$ and a nearby $p(\cdot\mid\theta+\Delta\theta)$ has a second-order Taylor expansion (the first-order term vanishes because $\mathrm{KL}(p_\theta\|p_\theta)=0$ is a minimum at $\Delta\theta=0$) given exactly by the Fisher information matrix:

$$
\mathrm{KL}\big(p_\theta \,\|\, p_{\theta+\Delta\theta}\big) \approx \frac{1}{2}\Delta\theta^\top \mathcal{I}(\theta)\,\Delta\theta
$$

This means $\mathcal{I}(\theta)$ acts as a local **Riemannian metric** on the space of distributions parameterized by $\theta$ — it tells you how much a small step $\Delta\theta$ actually moves the distribution, in KL terms, not just in raw Euclidean terms. **Natural gradient descent** replaces the ordinary gradient step $\theta \leftarrow \theta - \eta\nabla_\theta \mathcal{L}$ with a step that accounts for this geometry:

$$
\theta \leftarrow \theta - \eta\, \mathcal{I}(\theta)^{-1}\nabla_\theta\mathcal{L}(\theta)
$$

This is also exactly why Fisher information shows up in second-order optimization more broadly: since $\mathcal{I}(\theta) = -\mathbb{E}[\text{Hessian of }\log p]$, using $\mathcal{I}(\theta)^{-1}$ in place of the true inverse Hessian gives a **Gauss-Newton-like** update — it captures curvature information (rescaling steps to be small in flat/insensitive directions and appropriately larger in sharp/sensitive directions) without needing to compute or invert the full, possibly indefinite, Hessian directly. This is the theoretical basis for the Fisher information matrix as a practical, guaranteed-positive-semi-definite curvature approximation in optimizers like K-FAC and in the theory underlying natural policy gradients in reinforcement learning.

## Code Example

```python
import numpy as np
from scipy.stats import norm

rng = np.random.default_rng(0)

# --- Example: Fisher information for the mean of a Normal(mu, sigma^2), sigma known ---
sigma = 2.0
mu_true = 5.0
n = 2000
samples = rng.normal(mu_true, sigma, size=n)

# Analytical Fisher information (per observation) for a Normal mean:
# log p(x|mu) = -0.5*log(2*pi*sigma^2) - (x-mu)^2/(2*sigma^2)
# d/dmu log p = (x - mu) / sigma^2
# d^2/dmu^2 log p = -1/sigma^2  =>  I(mu) = 1/sigma^2 (per sample), exactly, no randomness needed here
I_analytical_per_sample = 1.0 / sigma**2

# Empirical check: I(mu) = Var(score) = E[score^2], evaluated at the true mu
score = (samples - mu_true) / sigma**2
I_empirical_per_sample = np.mean(score**2)

print("Analytical I(mu) per sample:", I_analytical_per_sample)
print("Empirical  I(mu) per sample:", I_empirical_per_sample)

# Total Fisher information for n i.i.d. samples scales linearly with n
I_total = n * I_analytical_per_sample
cramer_rao_bound = 1.0 / I_total
print("Cramer-Rao lower bound on Var(mu_hat):", cramer_rao_bound)

# The sample mean IS the MLE for mu here, and its true variance is sigma^2/n --
# which exactly equals the Cramer-Rao bound: the sample mean is fully efficient.
print("Actual Var(sample mean) = sigma^2/n =", sigma**2 / n)

# --- Numerically estimate Fisher information via finite-difference Hessian of log-likelihood ---
def loglik(mu, x):
    return np.sum(norm.logpdf(x, loc=mu, scale=sigma))

h = 1e-4
mu0 = mu_true
d2 = (loglik(mu0 + h, samples) - 2*loglik(mu0, samples) + loglik(mu0 - h, samples)) / h**2
I_from_hessian = -d2 / n   # negative expected Hessian, per-sample
print("Fisher info from finite-difference Hessian (per sample):", I_from_hessian)
```

## Common Mistakes

- **Confusing Fisher information with "information" in the Shannon sense.** Shannon entropy/information (see [Entropy & Cross-Entropy](/docs/foundations/information-theory/entropy-cross-entropy)) quantifies uncertainty in a distribution's outcomes; Fisher information quantifies how much a distribution's *shape* changes as its *parameters* change. They are related fields but answer fundamentally different questions and are not interchangeable.
- **Forgetting the regularity conditions.** The derivations above (score has zero mean, Fisher information equals negative expected Hessian) require being able to differentiate under the integral sign and require the support of $p(x\mid\theta)$ to not depend on $\theta$. Distributions with parameter-dependent support (e.g., $\mathrm{Uniform}(0,\theta)$) violate this, and the standard Fisher information machinery and Cramér-Rao bound do not directly apply.
- **Assuming the Cramér-Rao bound is always achievable.** It's a lower bound; not every unbiased estimator attains it for finite sample sizes. MLE is only asymptotically efficient (achieves the bound as $n\to\infty$), not necessarily for small samples.
- **Treating natural gradient descent as "just Newton's method."** Newton's method uses the actual Hessian of the loss (which can be indefinite, causing divergence at saddle points); natural gradient uses the Fisher information matrix, which is always positive semi-definite by construction (it's a covariance/second-moment matrix) — this is precisely why it's used as a more stable curvature approximation in practice.
- **Ignoring that Fisher information for i.i.d. samples is additive.** For $n$ i.i.d. observations, the total Fisher information is $n$ times the per-sample Fisher information (as in the code example) — a common error is using the per-sample value directly in the Cramér-Rao bound for a dataset of size $n$.
