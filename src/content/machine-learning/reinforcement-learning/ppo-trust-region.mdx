## Intuition

Vanilla policy gradient methods take a step $\theta \leftarrow \theta + \alpha \nabla_\theta J(\theta)$ using gradient information computed from data collected under the *current* policy. This creates a subtle but severe problem: the step size $\alpha$ controls movement in *parameter* space, but what actually matters is movement in *policy behavior* space — and the relationship between the two is wildly nonlinear. A small change in $\theta$ can leave the policy's action distribution almost untouched in one part of parameter space, or catastrophically flip it (e.g. turning a mostly-safe driving policy into one that floors the accelerator) in another. Because the training data was collected under the *old* policy, the estimated gradient direction is only trustworthy for a *small* neighborhood of policies around the old one — take too large a step, and you can jump to a policy so different that it performs far worse, and (worse still) that bad policy is now the one collecting the *next* batch of training data. There is no "undo" — a policy collapse can be difficult or impossible to recover from, unlike an SGD misstep in supervised learning where the next mislabeled gradient step usually can't hurt much because the data distribution is fixed.

**Trust region** methods address this directly: constrain each policy update so that the new policy stays within some bounded "distance" (measured by KL divergence) of the old policy, guaranteeing the update is conservative enough that the approximations underlying the policy gradient estimate remain valid. **TRPO** (Trust Region Policy Optimization) enforces this literally, as a constrained optimization problem, using second-order (Fisher information / natural gradient) machinery to solve it. **PPO** (Proximal Policy Optimization) approximates the same effect with a much simpler first-order trick: a clipped objective that removes the incentive to move too far, without ever explicitly constraining or measuring a KL divergence.

<Callout type="tip" title="The one idea that matters">
  PPO doesn't solve a constrained optimization problem — it *reshapes the objective* so that the policy has nothing to gain by moving too far from where the data was collected. Once the probability ratio between new and old policy exceeds a small window around 1, the clipped objective simply stops rewarding further movement in that direction, acting as a soft, built-in trust region.
</Callout>

## Formal Setup: The Surrogate Objective

Because we want to reuse data collected under an old policy $\pi_{\theta_{\text{old}}}$ to evaluate and improve a new policy $\pi_\theta$, we need an off-policy correction. Importance sampling lets us re-weight the expectation of any quantity under one distribution using samples from another:

$$
\mathbb{E}_{a \sim \pi_\theta}[f(a)] = \mathbb{E}_{a \sim \pi_{\theta_{\text{old}}}}\left[ \frac{\pi_\theta(a\mid s)}{\pi_{\theta_{\text{old}}}(a \mid s)} f(a) \right].
$$

Applying this to the policy gradient objective (using the advantage $A^{\pi_{\theta_\text{old}}}(s,a)$ in place of the raw return, as derived on the actor-critic page, since it's a lower-variance, equally valid gradient signal) gives the **surrogate objective**:

$$
L^{\text{CPI}}(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_{(s,a)\sim \pi_{\theta_{\text{old}}}} \left[ \frac{\pi_\theta(a\mid s)}{\pi_{\theta_{\text{old}}}(a\mid s)} \, A^{\pi_{\theta_{\text{old}}}}(s,a) \right].
$$

Define the **probability ratio** $r_t(\theta) = \dfrac{\pi_\theta(a_t\mid s_t)}{\pi_{\theta_{\text{old}}}(a_t\mid s_t)}$, so $r_t(\theta_{\text{old}}) = 1$ exactly at the old policy. The surrogate objective is a good local approximation to the true improvement in expected return near $\theta_{\text{old}}$ (this is the "conservative policy iteration" result underlying both TRPO and PPO) — but maximizing $L^{\text{CPI}}(\theta)$ *without any constraint* is dangerous: nothing stops $\theta$ from drifting to a point where $r_t(\theta)$ is enormous and the linear approximation is no longer valid, producing an update that looks good on the (invalid) surrogate but is actually catastrophic on the real objective.

## TRPO: Explicit Trust Region

TRPO makes the "stay close to the old policy" requirement literal, by maximizing the surrogate objective subject to a hard constraint on the average KL divergence between old and new policy:

$$
\max_\theta \; \mathbb{E}\left[ r_t(\theta) A_t \right] \quad \text{subject to} \quad \mathbb{E}_{s}\big[ D_{KL}(\pi_{\theta_{\text{old}}}(\cdot\mid s) \,\|\, \pi_\theta(\cdot\mid s)) \big] \le \delta.
$$

Solving this constrained problem requires approximating the KL constraint with its second-order (Fisher information matrix) expansion and using conjugate gradient plus a line search to find a step that improves the objective while respecting the constraint — correct, but computationally heavy and fiddly to implement (it needs Hessian-vector products and careful numerical safeguards).

## Derivation: PPO's Clipped Surrogate Objective

PPO's goal is to get *approximately* the same effect as TRPO's KL constraint — discourage large policy changes — using only first-order gradient information, no Hessians, no constrained optimization solver.

Start from the unclipped surrogate objective for a single sample, $r_t(\theta) A_t$. The problem case is when $A_t > 0$ (the action was better than average): maximizing $r_t(\theta) A_t$ pushes $r_t(\theta) \to \infty$ with no limit, i.e. it keeps increasing the probability of that action without bound, which is exactly the runaway-update failure mode. Symmetrically, when $A_t < 0$, maximizing $r_t(\theta)A_t$ pushes $r_t(\theta) \to 0$, driving the action's probability to zero, again without limit.

PPO's fix is to clip the ratio to a fixed interval $[1-\epsilon, 1+\epsilon]$ around 1 (typical $\epsilon = 0.2$), forming a second, clipped copy of the objective, and then take the **minimum** of the clipped and unclipped versions:

$$
L^{\text{CLIP}}(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_t \Big[ \min\big( r_t(\theta)\, A_t, \; \text{clip}(r_t(\theta), 1-\epsilon, 1+\epsilon)\, A_t \big) \Big].
$$

To see why the min with a clipped term removes the incentive to overshoot, consider the two cases separately:

**Case $A_t > 0$** (good action — we want to increase its probability, but only up to a point). For $r_t \le 1+\epsilon$, both terms inside the min are equal ($\text{clip}$ is inactive), so the objective behaves like the ordinary surrogate. Once $r_t > 1+\epsilon$, the clipped term flattens at $(1+\epsilon)A_t$ while the unclipped term $r_t A_t$ keeps growing — and since $A_t>0$, the *smaller* of the two is now the flat clipped term. Taking the min therefore caps the objective's value (and hence its gradient — the flat region has zero gradient with respect to $\theta$) once $r_t$ exceeds $1+\epsilon$: **there is no more benefit to pushing $r_t$ higher**, so gradient ascent stops rewarding further movement in that direction.

**Case $A_t < 0$** (bad action — we want to decrease its probability, but only up to a point). Here we care about the direction gradient ascent naturally moves $r_t$: since $A_t<0$, the unclipped term $r_t A_t$ is increased by *decreasing* $r_t$, so ascent pushes $r_t \to 0$ with no limit — the runaway case, mirroring $A_t>0$ but in the opposite direction. Once $r_t$ drops below $1-\epsilon$, the clipped term flattens at $(1-\epsilon)A_t$, a fixed (negative) number no longer sensitive to further decreases in $r_t$, while the unclipped term $r_t A_t$ keeps *increasing* as $r_t \to 0$ (a negative number times a shrinking-toward-zero positive ratio grows toward zero, i.e. increases). Since the clipped term is now the *smaller* of the two once $r_t<1-\epsilon$, the min selects it — again capping the objective and zeroing the gradient with respect to further decreases in $r_t$.

In both cases, the min operator selects whichever term is more pessimistic (lower), and that pessimistic term happens to be the flat, clipped one exactly once $r_t$ has moved past the boundary in the direction ascent was already pushing it. That's what removes the incentive to keep moving the policy further away from $\pi_{\theta_\text{old}}$ once the ratio has left the trusted $[1-\epsilon, 1+\epsilon]$ band.

The net effect: $L^{\text{CLIP}}$ behaves identically to the unclipped surrogate for small policy changes ($r_t$ near 1), but removes the *gradient signal* to keep changing the policy once the ratio has moved by more than $\epsilon$ — the policy simply stops being pushed further in that direction by that sample. This is a soft, per-sample, first-order surrogate for TRPO's hard, global, second-order KL constraint: no explicit KL divergence is ever computed, yet large, destabilizing steps are discouraged by construction.

## Code Example: Computing the PPO Clipped Objective

```python
import numpy as np

def ppo_clipped_objective(new_log_probs, old_log_probs, advantages, epsilon=0.2):
    """
    new_log_probs, old_log_probs: log pi_theta(a|s), log pi_theta_old(a|s) for a batch of (s,a) samples
    advantages: estimated A(s,a) for each sample (e.g. from GAE, using the critic)
    Returns the scalar PPO-CLIP loss to *minimize* (negative of the objective to maximize).
    """
    ratio = np.exp(new_log_probs - old_log_probs)          # r_t(theta), numerically stable via log-space subtraction
    unclipped = ratio * advantages
    clipped = np.clip(ratio, 1 - epsilon, 1 + epsilon) * advantages
    per_sample_objective = np.minimum(unclipped, clipped)   # pessimistic bound, per the derivation above
    objective = per_sample_objective.mean()
    loss = -objective                                        # gradient ASCENT on objective == gradient DESCENT on -objective
    return loss

# Sanity check: when the ratio is exactly 1 (theta == theta_old), clipping never activates
r = np.array([1.0, 1.0])
adv = np.array([2.0, -1.5])
assert np.isclose(ppo_clipped_objective(np.log(r), np.zeros_like(r), adv), -np.minimum(r*adv, r*adv).mean())
```

In a full implementation, `new_log_probs` are recomputed every epoch of optimization as $\theta$ changes (the policy is optimized for several epochs on the same batch of collected data before new trajectories are gathered), while `old_log_probs` and `advantages` are fixed at the values computed when the data was collected — this reuse of a batch for multiple gradient steps is exactly what makes clipping necessary: without it, several epochs of unclipped ascent on the same batch could push the ratio far from 1.

## Common Mistakes

<Callout type="warning" title="Pitfalls to avoid">
  - **Believing PPO explicitly computes or bounds a KL divergence.** It doesn't — the clipped ratio is a proxy that discourages large moves, but two policies can satisfy the clip bound while still differing substantially in KL divergence; some PPO variants add an explicit KL penalty term as a supplementary safeguard, but the core "clip" version does not.
  - **Forgetting why clipping alone is not enough without the min.** Clipping the ratio by itself, without the outer min against the unclipped term, does not fully prevent bad updates — the min is what ensures the *pessimistic* (lower) estimate is always used, so that the objective can't be improved by exploiting the region *between* the true objective and its clipped surrogate.
  - **Reusing a batch for too many optimization epochs.** While PPO's clipping tolerates multiple epochs on the same batch (unlike vanilla policy gradient), too many epochs still let the effective policy drift far from the data-collecting policy in aggregate, degrading performance — this is tuned via the number of epochs and minibatch size, not the clip parameter alone.
  - **Confusing PPO's ratio clipping with gradient clipping.** They are unrelated mechanisms — ratio clipping bounds the *policy probability ratio* inside the objective; gradient clipping (a separate, optional technique) bounds the norm of the *gradient vector* during optimization.
</Callout>
