## Intuition

No single model is universally best, but combining many models often beats every one of them individually — the question is *how* to combine them, and different combination strategies fix fundamentally different weaknesses. **Bagging** averages many independent, high-variance models to cancel out their individual noise. **Boosting** chains many weak, high-bias models together so each one specifically corrects what came before. **Stacking** trains a small model whose entire job is to learn how to best combine the predictions of several other, already-trained models.

<Callout type="tip" title="The core idea">
  Bagging trains models in parallel on resampled data and averages them to reduce variance; boosting trains models sequentially, each targeting the previous ensemble's errors, to reduce bias; stacking trains a meta-learner on the out-of-fold predictions of several base models to learn an optimal, possibly nonlinear, way to combine them. These are not competing techniques — they attack different terms in the bias-variance-noise decomposition.
</Callout>

## Formal Definitions

**Bagging (Bootstrap AGGregatING)**: draw $B$ bootstrap samples $D_1, \dots, D_B$ (each of size $n$, sampled with replacement from the original training set of size $n$), fit a base learner $h_b$ independently on each $D_b$, and combine by averaging (regression) or majority vote (classification):

$$
\hat f_{\text{bag}}(x) = \frac{1}{B}\sum_{b=1}^B h_b(x)
$$

Random forests extend bagging by also randomly subsampling features at each split, decorrelating the trees further (see [Decision Trees & Random Forests](/docs/machine-learning/supervised-learning/decision-trees-random-forests) (roadmap) for the full mechanism).

**Boosting**: build an additive ensemble sequentially, $F_m(x) = F_{m-1}(x) + \eta \, h_m(x)$, where each new weak learner $h_m$ is fit specifically to reduce the error remaining after $m-1$ rounds (e.g., to the negative gradient of the loss, in gradient boosting). See [Gradient Boosting](/docs/machine-learning/supervised-learning/gradient-boosting) for the full first- and second-order derivation.

**Stacking (Stacked Generalization, Wolpert 1992)**: train $K$ diverse base learners $h_1, \dots, h_K$ on the training data. Rather than combining their predictions with a fixed rule (average, vote), train a **meta-learner** $g$ whose inputs are the base learners' predictions and whose target is the original label:

$$
\hat f_{\text{stack}}(x) = g\big(h_1(x), h_2(x), \dots, h_K(x)\big)
$$

## Derivation: Why Bagging Reduces Variance

Suppose we have $B$ base models, each with the same variance $\sigma^2$ at a given query point, and pairwise correlation $\rho$ between any two models' predictions (correlation arises because they're trained on overlapping bootstrap samples of the same underlying data). The variance of their average is:

$$
\mathrm{Var}\left(\frac{1}{B}\sum_{b=1}^B h_b(x)\right) = \frac{1}{B^2}\left[\sum_{b=1}^B \mathrm{Var}(h_b(x)) + \sum_{b \neq b'} \mathrm{Cov}(h_b(x), h_{b'}(x))\right] = \rho\sigma^2 + \frac{1-\rho}{B}\sigma^2
$$

As $B \to \infty$, the second term vanishes and the variance of the ensemble converges to $\rho\sigma^2$ — strictly less than the individual model variance $\sigma^2$ whenever $\rho < 1$, but bounded below by $\rho \sigma^2$ no matter how large $B$ gets. This is exactly why random forests' extra feature-subsampling step matters: it further decorrelates the trees (lowers $\rho$) beyond what bootstrap resampling alone achieves, since bootstrap samples of the same dataset are still fairly similar to each other (typically only about 63.2% of the original rows appear in any given bootstrap sample, but the overlap across different bootstrap samples is still substantial). Crucially, averaging does not change the *bias* of the ensemble — $\mathbb{E}[\hat f_{\text{bag}}(x)] = \mathbb{E}[h_b(x)]$ for any single $b$ — so bagging is a pure variance-reduction technique that works best when the base learner is already low-bias but high-variance (e.g., a deep, unpruned decision tree).

## Derivation: Why Boosting Reduces Bias

Boosting starts from a base learner that is deliberately weak (high bias, low variance — e.g., a shallow decision stump) and builds an additive sequence $F_m = F_{m-1} + \eta h_m$ where $h_m$ is fit to the residual/gradient of $F_{m-1}$'s current errors (the full first- and second-order derivation is in [Gradient Boosting](/docs/machine-learning/supervised-learning/gradient-boosting)). Each additional round can only further reduce the *training* loss the previous rounds failed to capture — the ensemble's representational capacity grows with $m$, directly attacking the systematic error (bias) that a single weak learner alone could never eliminate. This is the opposite lever from bagging: boosting's base learners are individually high-bias/low-variance, and sequential correction drives bias down round by round, at the cost of variance and overfitting risk if $m$ grows too large or $\eta$ is too high — which is exactly why boosting implementations expose shrinkage ($\eta$), early stopping, and row/column subsampling as variance-control mechanisms bolted onto a fundamentally bias-reducing procedure.

## Derivation: Why Stacking Needs Out-of-Fold Predictions

The naive approach — train each base learner $h_k$ on the full training set, generate their predictions on that same training set, and train the meta-learner $g$ on those predictions — is subtly but seriously flawed. Each $h_k(x_i)$ for a training point $x_i$ is generated by a model that *saw $x_i$'s label during training*, so $h_k(x_i)$ is an overly optimistic, low-error prediction relative to what $h_k$ would produce on a genuinely unseen point. The meta-learner $g$, trained on these artificially accurate inputs, learns to trust each base model's predictions *more* than it should — a form of the same overfitting/leakage that motivates held-out validation everywhere else in this section.

The fix is to generate the base models' training-time predictions the same way cross-validation generates honest validation scores: partition the training data into $K$ folds, and for each fold $D_i$, train $h_k$ on $D \setminus D_i$ and predict on $D_i$. Stitching these **out-of-fold (OOF) predictions** together across all $K$ folds gives, for every training point, a base-model prediction generated by a version of $h_k$ that never saw that point during training — exactly analogous to a validation-fold prediction in ordinary $k$-fold CV. The meta-learner $g$ is then trained on these OOF predictions, so it learns the true, held-out-quality relationship between base-model outputs and the label, rather than an inflated one. At prediction time on genuinely new data, each $h_k$ is refit on the *entire* training set (all folds) before producing the input that $g$ consumes.

## Code Example

```python
import numpy as np
from sklearn.ensemble import (
    BaggingClassifier, RandomForestClassifier, GradientBoostingClassifier,
    StackingClassifier,
)
from sklearn.linear_model import LogisticRegression
from sklearn.svm import SVC
from sklearn.tree import DecisionTreeClassifier
from sklearn.model_selection import cross_val_score
from sklearn.datasets import make_classification

X, y = make_classification(n_samples=2000, n_features=20, random_state=0)

# Bagging: parallel, variance reduction. Base learner is deliberately unpruned (low bias, high variance).
bagging = BaggingClassifier(
    estimator=DecisionTreeClassifier(),  # no max_depth cap -> high variance base learner
    n_estimators=200, max_samples=0.8, random_state=0,
)
rf = RandomForestClassifier(n_estimators=300, random_state=0)  # bagging + feature subsampling

# Boosting: sequential, bias reduction. Base learner is deliberately weak (shallow).
boosting = GradientBoostingClassifier(
    n_estimators=300, max_depth=2, learning_rate=0.05, random_state=0,
)

# Stacking: meta-learner trained on out-of-fold base-model predictions.
# StackingClassifier internally uses cross_val_predict to generate OOF predictions
# for the base estimators before training the final_estimator -- this is what
# prevents the leakage described above.
stacking = StackingClassifier(
    estimators=[
        ("logreg", LogisticRegression(max_iter=1000)),
        ("svc", SVC(probability=True)),
        ("tree", DecisionTreeClassifier(max_depth=5)),
    ],
    final_estimator=LogisticRegression(),
    cv=5,  # controls the OOF-prediction generation
)

for name, model in [("Bagging", bagging), ("RandomForest", rf), ("Boosting", boosting), ("Stacking", stacking)]:
    score = cross_val_score(model, X, y, cv=5, scoring="roc_auc").mean()
    print(f"{name:14s} ROC-AUC: {score:.4f}")
```

## Common Mistakes

<Callout type="warning" title="Frequent errors">
  - **Bagging an already low-variance, high-bias base learner (e.g., a shallow decision stump or linear model) and expecting a big improvement.** Since bagging's variance-reduction formula shows the ensemble variance is bounded by rho*sigma^2, there is little variance to reduce from a base learner that already has low variance — bagging works best on unstable, high-variance learners like unpruned trees.
  - **Boosting for too many rounds without early stopping or a validation-based stopping criterion.** Because each round adds representational capacity, boosting can eventually overfit the training set; monitor validation loss and stop (or tune `n_estimators`/`learning_rate` jointly) rather than assuming more rounds is always better.
  - **Building a stacked ensemble using in-sample (not out-of-fold) base-model predictions to train the meta-learner.** As derived above, this leaks information and causes the meta-learner to overtrust base models, typically producing an ensemble that looks great in training but underperforms in deployment relative to a properly-stacked one.
  - **Assuming random forests eliminate the need for hyperparameter tuning entirely.** They are robust to many settings by default, but `max_depth`, `min_samples_leaf`, and the number of features sampled per split (`max_features`) still meaningfully affect the bias-variance balance and correlation between trees.
  - **Ensembling many highly correlated models and expecting bagging-like gains.** If the base models make very similar errors (e.g., several near-identical gradient boosting models with slightly different seeds), rho stays high and the variance-reduction benefit is small — diversity among base learners (different algorithms, feature subsets, or training data) is what makes ensembling (especially stacking) pay off.
</Callout>
